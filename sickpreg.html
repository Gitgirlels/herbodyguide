Getting Sick While Pregnant: Colds, Flu, Fever & More

Every pregnant woman will likely experience at least one illness during her nine months — and for good reason. Pregnancy involves profound and deliberate changes to the immune system that alter susceptibility to certain infections while protecting the developing fetus. Understanding why illness behaves differently during pregnancy, which symptoms demand urgent attention, what treatments are safe, and how to prevent the most serious complications is essential knowledge for every expectant mother. This guide incorporates the latest evidence through April 2026, including ACOG's August and September 2025 Practice Advisories on influenza and paracetamol, CDC 2024–2025 flu season data, updated RSV vaccine guidance, and the 2025 ACOG COVID-19 antiviral recommendations.

Why Pregnancy Changes How You Get Sick

The Pregnant Immune System: A Deliberate Recalibration

A common misconception is that pregnancy simply suppresses the immune system across the board. The reality is far more nuanced. Pregnancy requires the immune system to perform an extraordinary balancing act: tolerating a genetically foreign fetus (which carries paternal antigens the maternal immune system would normally attack) while simultaneously defending against genuine pathogens.

The Immunological Shift of Pregnancy

Regulatory T cells (Tregs) expand dramatically during pregnancy, dampening the adaptive immune responses that would otherwise reject fetal tissue. Simultaneously, innate immune responses — the body's first-line, non-specific defenses — are upregulated at the uterine interface. This shift is governed by rising progesterone, which promotes immune tolerance and suppresses Th1 (cell-mediated) responses, while the placenta secretes immunomodulatory hormones including hCG, human placental lactogen, and estradiol. The net result is reduced cell-mediated immunity — the arm of the immune system most critical for fighting intracellular pathogens like viruses — with relatively preserved humoral (antibody-mediated) immunity.

This modulation is not uniform across all nine months. The first trimester requires maximal immune tolerance and is most immunologically modified. The second trimester is more stable. The third trimester shifts back toward a pro-inflammatory profile to prepare for labour — and carries the greatest risk of severe respiratory illness complications.

Physiological Changes That Amplify Illness Symptoms

Respiratory and Cardiovascular Changes

Airway mucosa: Rising estrogen and progesterone cause mucosal congestion throughout the respiratory tract, explaining "pregnancy rhinitis" — chronic nasal congestion independent of any infection. Even mild viral upper respiratory infections compound this, causing more significant congestion than in non-pregnant women.

Reduced lung reserve: The expanding uterus elevates the diaphragm by up to 4 cm, reducing functional residual capacity by approximately 20%. Oxygen consumption simultaneously increases by about 20% due to fetal and placental metabolic demands. Pregnant women therefore have less respiratory reserve and become hypoxic more rapidly when respiratory illness impairs lung function.

Cardiovascular burden: Cardiac output increases 30–50% during pregnancy. Any infection raising metabolic demand adds further strain to an already-taxed cardiovascular system — explaining why influenza and pneumonia carry substantially higher complication rates in pregnancy than in non-pregnant adults of equivalent age.

The Common Cold During Pregnancy

Causes, Severity, and Fetal Risk

The common cold is caused by over 200 viral strains — rhinoviruses account for 30–50% of cases, followed by adenoviruses, coronaviruses (non-SARS-CoV-2), and parainfluenza viruses. Pregnancy does not specifically increase susceptibility to cold viruses, but symptoms often feel more severe due to the physiological changes above, particularly the compounding of viral mucosal inflammation onto pre-existing pregnancy rhinitis.

The common cold does not cross the placenta and does not directly infect the fetus. Rhinovirus and the other cold-causing viruses are not associated with structural birth defects. The primary fetal risk from a simple cold is indirect — through high fever — which is why fever management is taken seriously even when the underlying illness appears mild. A cold with no fever poses minimal risk to the developing baby.

Cold duration mirrors non-pregnancy: most symptoms resolve within 7–10 days, with congestion and cough possibly persisting up to 2 weeks. Medical evaluation is warranted if symptoms worsen significantly after day 5–7, new features develop (high fever, productive coloured sputum, facial pain, significant shortness of breath), or symptoms fail to resolve by day 14 — to exclude sinusitis, otitis media, or pneumonia.

Influenza (Flu) During Pregnancy 2024–25 DATA

Context: The 2024–2025 Flu Season

The 2024–2025 influenza season provides stark, current context for why influenza is taken so seriously in pregnancy. It was the most severe US flu season since 2017–2018, and the data on pregnant women are alarming.

2024–2025 US flu season (CDC, September 2025 and March 2026 reports):

An estimated 51 million influenza-related illnesses, 710,000 hospitalizations, and 45,000 deaths nationwide. The cumulative influenza-associated hospitalization rate reached 127.1–161.5 per 100,000 population — the highest since at least 2010–2011. Hospitalization rates across all age groups were 1.8–2.8 times higher than median historical rates.

Among 4,135 hospitalized women of childbearing age (15–49 years) with known pregnancy status, 22.6% were pregnant — a striking overrepresentation. Flu vaccination is estimated to have prevented approximately 10 million illnesses and 180,000 hospitalizations nationwide, reinforcing the clinical importance of maternal vaccination. The 2024–25 season also saw approximately 790 flu-related pediatric deaths — the highest for any regular flu season since tracking began — highlighting the community-wide severity that directly affected pregnant women and their households.

Why Flu Is Systematically More Dangerous in Pregnancy

Mechanisms of Increased Severity

Reduced cell-mediated immunity: Influenza is controlled primarily through cytotoxic T lymphocyte responses — precisely the arm of immunity most suppressed in pregnancy. Viral replication is less effectively controlled, leading to higher viral loads and more prolonged illness.

Cytokine dysregulation: The innate immune response to influenza involves a rapid cytokine surge. In pregnancy, some cytokines that would normally limit viral spread are suppressed for fetal tolerance. This disrupted antiviral response allows the virus to establish deeper respiratory tract infection.

Reduced respiratory reserve: Influenza pneumonia impairs oxygen diffusion at a time when pregnant women already operate on reduced reserve and higher oxygen demand. Hypoxia that a non-pregnant woman might tolerate can cause fetal distress, particularly in the third trimester.

Flu Complications in Pregnancy by Trimester

First trimester: High fever raises risk of neural tube defects and structural abnormalities during organogenesis (weeks 4–10). Dehydration is amplified by concurrent morning sickness, making fluid management critical.

Second trimester: Risk of preterm labour increases with febrile illness. Flu complicated by pneumonia is associated with preterm birth rates of 30–40% in some study cohorts. Influenza B may carry particular complications in this trimester.

Third trimester: The highest-risk period for severe maternal illness due to maximal physiological burden. Markedly elevated risk of ICU admission, preterm birth, fetal distress, and emergency caesarean compared to non-pregnant women of similar age.

Cold vs. Flu: How to Tell the Difference

Clinical Differentiation

Onset: Colds develop gradually over 1–3 days. Influenza begins abruptly — many patients identify the exact hour they became unwell.

Fever: Colds rarely cause high fever. Influenza typically produces 38–40°C (100.4–104°F) fever within the first 24 hours, lasting 3–5 days.

Systemic symptoms: The hallmark of influenza is severe systemic illness — myalgia, arthralgia, profound headache, and fatigue qualitatively different from cold-related tiredness. "Hit by a bus" describes it clinically.

Respiratory symptoms: Both cause nasal symptoms, but influenza typically produces an early dry cough. Nasal congestion is generally more prominent in a cold.

Testing note: Rapid influenza tests have only 50–70% sensitivity. A negative test does NOT rule out influenza in a pregnant woman with a compatible clinical picture during flu season. Per 2025 ACOG and CDC guidance, clinical judgment — not test results — should drive antiviral prescribing decisions in pregnant women.

Antiviral Treatment: Oseltamivir in Pregnancy 2025 GUIDANCE

Current ACOG/CDC Recommendation (August 2025 Practice Advisory)

Oseltamivir (Tamiflu) is safe in all trimesters and is the recommended antiviral for influenza in pregnancy. The 2025 ACOG Practice Advisory states explicitly: do not wait for confirmatory test results before prescribing oseltamivir to a pregnant woman with suspected influenza. Treatment is most effective within 48 hours of symptom onset but should still be offered after this window in severe or progressive illness. Standard dose: 75 mg twice daily for 5 days. No increase in birth defects has been observed in pregnancy registries.

Post-exposure chemoprophylaxis: If you have had close contact with a confirmed influenza case, ACOG 2025 recommends considering oseltamivir 75 mg once daily for 10 days for pregnant women and those within 2 weeks postpartum. If oseltamivir is unavailable, zanamivir may be substituted at two inhalations once daily for 10 days. All women who are pregnant or within 2 weeks postpartum should call for evaluation immediately if fever above 37.8°C (100°F) develops alongside shortness of breath, chest pain, or syncope.

Fever During Pregnancy

Why Fever Requires Active Management

Fever at or above 38°C (100.4°F) warrants prompt evaluation and treatment regardless of the suspected cause. Unlike non-pregnant adults where mild fever is sometimes left untreated to support immune function, the fetal risks of untreated fever clearly outweigh any theoretical immune benefit.

Specific Fetal Risks of Untreated Fever

Teratogenicity in early pregnancy: Hyperthermia during weeks 4–14 is associated with neural tube defects, conotruncal heart defects, and facial clefting. The mechanism involves heat-induced apoptosis of differentiating neural crest cells at temperatures above approximately 39°C.

Neurodevelopmental risk: Research shows maternal fever in the second trimester is associated with an approximately 40% increased risk of autism spectrum disorder in offspring; fever after the 12th week of pregnancy increased the risk approximately 3-fold in some analyses. Critically, these associations appear to be with the fever itself — not with its treatment. Studies indicate that antipyretic use to control fever substantially attenuates the increased neurodevelopmental risk, which is a key reason why treating fever in pregnancy — rather than waiting it out — is the evidence-based recommendation.

Preterm labour: Fever triggers prostaglandin release, the same compounds that initiate labour contractions. Febrile illness in mid-to-late pregnancy raises preterm labour risk regardless of the cause.

Fetal tachycardia: Fetal heart rate increases approximately 10 beats per minute for each 1°C rise in maternal temperature. Sustained fever can drive rates into ranges associated with fetal distress.

The Paracetamol Controversy Resolved SEPT 2025

In September 2025, the US FDA initiated a label change suggesting prenatal acetaminophen use might be associated with autism and ADHD — triggering widespread concern. The obstetric and scientific communities responded swiftly with a clear, evidence-based clarification.

ACOG September 2025 Practice Advisory: What the Evidence Shows

ACOG issued a formal Practice Advisory directly responding to the FDA label change, stating: "Acetaminophen remains the safest first-line analgesic and antipyretic in pregnancy. The current weight of evidence does not support a causal link between prenatal acetaminophen use and neurodevelopmental disorders. No change in clinical practice is warranted."

This position is grounded in the best available evidence — a Swedish population-based study published in JAMA in April 2024 (Ahlqvist et al.) analysing 2.48 million children born 1995–2019. Using a sibling-controlled design — the gold standard for controlling shared genetics and family environment — this study found no significant association between prenatal acetaminophen use and autism (HR 0.98), ADHD (HR 0.98), or intellectual disability (HR 1.01). ACOG's review found that most studies suggesting an association had significant methodological flaws including recall bias and failure to account for genetic confounding. A 2026 Lancet systematic review and meta-analysis reached the same conclusion: when studies with adequate confounder control are examined, the evidence for a causal link is not established.

The RCOG (UK), FIGO, the European Medicines Agency, and RANZCOG all continue to recommend paracetamol as the first-choice antipyretic in pregnancy. The clinical bottom line: the well-established risks of untreated fever in pregnancy outweigh any unproven theoretical risk from appropriate short-term paracetamol use. Use the lowest effective dose for the shortest necessary duration.

Fever Medications to Avoid in Pregnancy

Ibuprofen and NSAIDs (naproxen, aspirin in anti-inflammatory doses) should be avoided, particularly after 20 weeks. NSAIDs can cause premature closure of the ductus arteriosus, impair fetal renal function causing oligohydramnios, and are associated with increased miscarriage risk in early pregnancy. The FDA advises avoidance from 20 weeks onward; they are contraindicated after 30 weeks. Note: low-dose aspirin (100–150 mg/day) is actually prescribed to prevent preeclampsia in high-risk pregnancies — a completely distinct clinical context.

Codeine-containing preparations should not be self-administered for fever. Near term, neonatal respiratory depression is a risk.

Safe Cold Medicines in Pregnancy

Generally Considered Safe

Paracetamol (Panadol/Tylenol): Safe for fever and pain throughout all trimesters at recommended doses (500–1,000 mg every 4–6 hours, maximum 4,000 mg/24 hours). See above for the 2025 ACOG evidence update clarifying the neurodevelopmental concerns.

Saline nasal sprays and rinses: The safest and most evidence-supported first-line treatment for nasal congestion throughout pregnancy — zero systemic absorption, effective, and beneficial for pregnancy rhinitis independent of infection.

Steam inhalation and humidifiers: Safe non-pharmacological approaches to relieve congestion and ease cough. External topical menthol products (Vicks VapoRub on chest) are generally safe for external use; avoid intranasal application.

Honey: Randomised controlled trial evidence supports honey as an effective cough suppressant comparable to dextromethorphan in adults, with an established safe pregnancy profile. Honey and warm lemon is a well-supported home remedy.

Throat lozenges: Lozenges containing benzydamine, glycerine, or honey are considered safe. Avoid aspirin-containing or iodine-based antiseptic lozenges.

Hydration and rest: Universally recommended and genuinely evidence-based. Dehydration in pregnancy can trigger Braxton-Hicks contractions and, in severe cases, preterm labour.

Use With Caution or Seek Advice Before Using

Oral decongestants (pseudoephedrine, phenylephrine): Cause vasoconstriction that may reduce uteroplacental blood flow. Some studies link first-trimester pseudoephedrine to gastroschisis. Most guidelines recommend avoiding oral decongestants in the first trimester; use later should be brief and on medical advice. Topical oxymetazoline nasal sprays (Drixine, Afrin) are preferred over oral formulations if a decongestant is truly needed — minimal systemic absorption. Do not use topical decongestants more than 3–5 consecutive days due to rebound congestion.

Antihistamines: First-generation antihistamines (diphenhydramine/Benadryl, chlorpheniramine) should be used cautiously and avoided near term due to risk of neonatal sedation. Second-generation antihistamines (loratadine, cetirizine) have a more favourable profile and are generally preferred when an antihistamine is necessary.

Cough suppressants (dextromethorphan): Generally considered acceptable for short-term use after the first trimester based on available data, though studies are limited. Avoid codeine-containing cough preparations.

Herbal and supplement remedies: Echinacea, elderberry, high-dose zinc, and high-dose vitamin C supplements all lack adequate pregnancy safety data. Do not assume "natural" means safe — avoid without specific medical advice.

COVID-19 During Pregnancy 2024–2025 EVIDENCE

What the Latest Evidence Shows

A large and now mature body of evidence has accumulated on COVID-19 in pregnancy. The most comprehensive recent data comes from the UK UKOSS national cohort study published in Nature Communications in April 2024, covering 16,627 pregnant women hospitalised across four SARS-CoV-2 variant waves from March 2020 to March 2022.

The 2024 UK national cohort (Engjom et al.) found that severe perinatal outcomes were significantly more common with moderate-to-severe COVID-19, during the Delta-dominant period, and — most importantly — among unvaccinated women. Vaccination consistently and substantially reduced adverse outcomes across all four variant periods studied. A 2024 systematic review of SARS-CoV-2 infection in early pregnancy (Acta Obstet Gynecol Scand) found no statistically significant association with miscarriage in meta-analysis, though individual studies reported inconsistent findings for stillbirth and preterm birth. Vertical transmission (direct fetal infection) is now understood to be rare. Current Mayo Clinic and ACOG guidance (October–September 2025) confirms that pregnancy raises the risk of severe COVID-19 and that this elevated risk persists for at least one month after birth.

Long COVID in Pregnancy — Emerging 2025 Evidence

A 2025 systematic review in Biomedical Reports (Georgakopoulou et al.) synthesising 13 studies covering 13,729 participants found that pregnant women with moderate-to-severe COVID-19 are at highest risk for persistent Long COVID symptoms — including fatigue, dyspnoea, and cognitive symptoms lasting beyond 4 weeks. Psychological symptoms such as anxiety and depression were prominent across studies. The 2025 ACOG Practice Advisory explicitly cites evidence that Paxlovid reduces the risk of Long COVID as a reason to offer it to eligible pregnant patients.

COVID-19 Treatment During Pregnancy 2025 ACOG GUIDANCE

Paxlovid (Nirmatrelvir/Ritonavir) — 2025 ACOG Recommendation

ACOG's updated 2025 Practice Advisory on COVID-19 in Pregnancy states that obstetric care providers should discuss and offer Paxlovid to pregnant patients with confirmed SARS-CoV-2 infection to mitigate severity and reduce the risk of Long COVID. This represents an evolution from earlier, more cautious guidance on Paxlovid in pregnancy. Standard dose (normal renal function): nirmatrelvir 300 mg (two 150 mg tablets) plus ritonavir 100 mg, all three tablets together twice daily for 5 days. Dose adjustments apply for moderate renal impairment. Ritonavir is a potent CYP3A4 inhibitor — clinicians must review all concurrent medications for drug–drug interactions before prescribing.

Hospitalised patients: Remdesivir remains the preferred inpatient antiviral for moderate-to-severe disease in pregnancy. Dexamethasone is standard of care for severe COVID-19 requiring supplemental oxygen and is well-established as safe in pregnancy.

COVID-19 Vaccination: 2025–2026 Recommendations

Updated COVID-19 vaccines for the 2025–2026 season (Moderna, Pfizer-BioNTech, Novavax) are recommended throughout pregnancy. Staying up to date on COVID-19 vaccination is recommended by the CDC, ACOG, and SMFM. A 2024 living systematic review and meta-analysis in Drug Safety (Ciapponi et al.) — covering 120 observational studies and randomised trials — confirmed the safety and effectiveness of COVID-19 vaccination during pregnancy, finding no credible evidence of vaccine-related fetal harm and significant maternal protection.

RSV During Pregnancy UPDATED 2025

Maternal RSV Vaccination: 2025–2026 Guidelines

RSV is the leading cause of infant hospitalisation in the US, with almost half of RSV-related infant hospitalisations occurring in babies under 3 months — before direct infant vaccination is possible. Maternal vaccination passes protective antibodies to the newborn before birth.

Current Abrysvo (Pfizer) Guidance — Updated September 2025

Who: Single dose of Abrysvo (Pfizer's RSVpreF vaccine) for eligible pregnant women at 32–36 weeks gestation. Abrysvo is the only RSV vaccine approved for use in pregnancy — GSK's Arexvy and Moderna's mResvia are NOT approved for pregnant women.

When: September through January in most of the US (timing varies by region based on local RSV seasonality). Co-administration with influenza, COVID-19, and Tdap vaccines is acceptable with no minimum waiting interval.

Efficacy: Phase 3 trial data confirm 68% reduction in infant hospitalisation for RSV and 57% reduction in RSV-related healthcare visits within 3 months of birth.

Important January 2025 safety update: The FDA required labelling changes noting an increased risk of Guillain-Barré syndrome (GBS) — estimated at 9 excess cases per million doses — but this risk was identified only in adults 65 years and older. ACOG explicitly states this update does not apply to pregnant women and that the benefits continue to outweigh risks in the pregnancy population.

Subsequent pregnancies: Women who received Abrysvo during a previous pregnancy should NOT be revaccinated in future pregnancies. Instead, their newborn should receive a monoclonal antibody — nirsevimab or clesrovimab — directly after birth for infant RSV protection. Most infants do not need both maternal vaccine and infant antibody.

Other Respiratory Illnesses in Pregnancy

Bacterial Pneumonia

Pneumonia — infection of the lung parenchyma — occurs in approximately 1–2 per 1,000 pregnancies and is among the most common serious non-obstetric infections requiring hospitalisation during pregnancy. The same physiological factors amplifying flu severity apply here. Community-acquired pneumonia in pregnancy is associated with preterm labour in 30–50% of cases. Most common bacterial causes: Streptococcus pneumoniae, Mycoplasma pneumoniae, and Haemophilus influenzae. Pregnancy-safe first-line antibiotics include amoxicillin-clavulanate and azithromycin. Fluoroquinolones and tetracyclines (including doxycycline) should be avoided during pregnancy.

Strep Throat (Group A Streptococcal Pharyngitis)

Confirmed GAS throat infection in pregnancy warrants antibiotic treatment to prevent post-streptococcal complications. Penicillin V and amoxicillin are safe first-line options throughout all trimesters. For penicillin-allergic women, azithromycin is an alternative, though GAS resistance is increasing. Note that Group B Streptococcus (GBS) — a related but distinct organism — is routinely screened at 35–37 weeks because of its role in neonatal sepsis; any discussion of strep in pregnancy should prompt clarification of which organism is involved.

The Four Recommended Vaccines in Pregnancy 2025–2026

In October 2024, the Maternal Immunization Task Force — including ACOG, SMFM, AAP, and the CDC — issued a joint Call to Action urging obstetric care providers to counsel all pregnant patients about the four currently recommended vaccines. These represent the most evidence-supported tools available for protecting both mother and newborn.

Four Recommended Vaccines During Pregnancy (2025–2026 Season)

1. Influenza (any trimester, annually): Inactivated quadrivalent vaccine as soon as available each season. Reduces maternal flu illness by approximately 50% and protects newborns for several months through transplacental antibody transfer. Live attenuated intranasal flu vaccine is NOT recommended during pregnancy. Particularly critical given the high-severity 2024–2025 season data above.

2. COVID-19 (any trimester, stay up to date): Updated 2025–2026 vaccines recommended. Evidence confirms significant reduction in ICU admission risk and improved infant outcomes in vaccinated versus unvaccinated infected women.

3. RSV — Abrysvo only (32–36 weeks, September–January): Single dose per pregnancy, seasonally administered, to protect the infant during their first 6 months. See updated 2025 guidance and GBS safety note above.

4. Tdap (27–36 weeks, ideally 27–32 weeks, every pregnancy): Tetanus, diphtheria, and acellular pertussis vaccine recommended in every pregnancy — not just the first. Maximises transplacental transfer of pertussis antibodies to protect newborns before they can receive direct vaccination at 6 weeks. All four vaccines can be co-administered at the same visit.

Vaccination coverage gap (CDC, April 2024 surveillance): Influenza vaccination coverage among pregnant women was approximately 47% for the 2023–2024 season — well below targets for adequate population protection. COVID-19 updated vaccine coverage in pregnancy was similarly suboptimal. Multiple studies including Regan et al. (2023 and 2024) have found no association between influenza vaccination during pregnancy and miscarriage, addressing one of the most common sources of vaccine hesitancy in pregnant women.

When to Seek Emergency Care

Red Flag Symptoms Requiring Immediate Medical Attention

Contact your midwife, obstetrician, or emergency services immediately if you experience any of the following during pregnancy:

Fever above 39°C (102.2°F) that does not respond to paracetamol within 1–2 hours

Difficulty breathing or shortness of breath at rest — distinct from the breathlessness of late pregnancy occurring with exertion

Chest pain or tightness, particularly if worse with deep breathing

Oxygen saturation below 95% on home pulse oximeter

Resting heart rate above 120 bpm

Confusion, severe headache, or altered consciousness

Severe dehydration: inability to retain any fluids, no urination for 8+ hours, severe postural dizziness

Reduced or absent fetal movements during any acute illness from approximately 28 weeks

Regular uterine contractions before 37 weeks of gestation

Vaginal bleeding, fluid leakage, or significant pelvic pressure associated with fever or acute illness

Per ACOG 2025 guidance: all pregnant women or women within 2 weeks postpartum should call immediately for evaluation if fever above 37.8°C (100°F) develops alongside shortness of breath, syncope, or chest pain.

Fetal Movement Monitoring During Illness

Fetal Movement Monitoring from 28 Weeks

From approximately 28 weeks, a healthy fetus typically makes at least 10 distinct movements in any 2-hour period during a time when it is normally active. During any acute illness, if you notice a decrease or change in fetal movements — even without counting — contact your maternity care provider the same day. Lie on your left side and count movements until you reach 10. If 10 are not felt within 2 hours, seek same-day assessment. Do not wait for the next routine appointment.

Fetal Risk Summary by Illness Type

Illness-by-Illness Fetal Risk Overview

Common cold (rhinovirus, adenovirus): Minimal direct fetal risk. Primary concern is high fever in early pregnancy. No direct fetal infection. No birth defect association.

Influenza: No direct teratogenicity. Fever-mediated risk in first trimester; preterm birth risk in second and third trimester. The high-severity 2024–2025 season (22.6% of hospitalised childbearing-age women were pregnant) demonstrates continued significant risk.

COVID-19 (SARS-CoV-2): Rare vertical transmission. Placental inflammation possible with moderate-to-severe disease. Preterm birth elevated with severe illness. Vaccination substantially reduces adverse perinatal outcomes. Long COVID a growing concern in those with moderate-to-severe illness during pregnancy.

RSV: RSV disease in the newborn after birth is the primary concern rather than fetal harm in utero. Maternal Abrysvo vaccination protects the newborn via antibody transfer.

Bacterial pneumonia: No direct fetal infection, but maternal hypoxia and systemic illness create substantial indirect risk including preterm birth and fetal distress.

Quick Reference: Illness Management at a Glance

Scenario-Based Action Guide

Runny nose and sore throat, no fever: Saline nasal rinse, honey and warm lemon, non-aspirin throat lozenges, rest and hydration. No medication usually required.

Fever 37.5–38.5°C: Paracetamol 500–1,000 mg, increase fluids, rest. Monitor every 2–3 hours. Contact midwife if not improving or if fever rises above 38.5°C.

Fever above 38.5°C / suspected influenza: Paracetamol immediately; contact your doctor or maternity care provider same day. Per 2025 ACOG/CDC guidance, oseltamivir (Tamiflu) should be offered without waiting for test results.

Positive COVID-19 test: Contact your obstetric provider promptly. Discuss eligibility for Paxlovid per 2025 ACOG guidance to reduce severity and Long COVID risk.

Productive cough, coloured sputum, chest pain, or breathlessness: Same-day medical evaluation. Do not self-treat suspected pneumonia.

Any fever with reduced fetal movements: Do not wait. Go to hospital or call your maternity unit immediately.

Unable to retain fluids for 8+ hours: Contact care provider — IV fluid replacement may be needed, as dehydration can trigger contractions.
