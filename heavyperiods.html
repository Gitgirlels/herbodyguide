Heavy Periods (Menorrhagia): The Complete Scientific Guide

Heavy menstrual bleeding — clinically termed menorrhagia or, within the broader classification of abnormal uterine bleeding (AUB) — affects approximately one in five women of reproductive age and represents one of the most common reasons women seek gynaecological care worldwide.
This comprehensive scientific guide examines the physiology of normal menstruation, the molecular and structural mechanisms underlying heavy menstrual bleeding, evidence-based diagnostic approaches, the full spectrum of medical and surgical treatment options, and the substantial but often underappreciated impact of heavy periods on quality of life, productivity, and systemic health.

Normal Menstrual Physiology

The Endometrial Cycle

Menstruation represents the shedding of the functionalis layer of the endometrium — the inner lining of the uterus — following the decline in progesterone that occurs when a fertilised egg fails to implant. Understanding normal menstrual biology is essential for comprehending the mechanisms that drive pathological bleeding.

Phases of the Endometrial Cycle

The endometrial cycle is divided into three phases: the proliferative phase (days 1–14 of a typical 28-day cycle), driven by rising oestrogen from developing ovarian follicles; the secretory phase (days 15–28), dominated by progesterone from the corpus luteum; and the menstrual phase, initiated by progesterone withdrawal. During the proliferative phase, oestrogen stimulates glandular and stromal proliferation, rebuilding the endometrium from the basalis layer following the previous menstruation. The secretory phase transforms the endometrium into a receptive environment rich in glycogen-secreting glands and coiled spiral arterioles in preparation for potential implantation.

Normal menstrual blood loss ranges from 5 to 80 mL per cycle, with a clinical and research threshold of 80 mL historically used to define menorrhagia. The average blood loss is approximately 35–40 mL per cycle. Women with menorrhagia lose more than 80 mL per cycle — often substantially more — though measurement of actual blood loss is impractical in routine clinical settings. Studies using the alkaline haematin extraction method demonstrate that approximately 30% of women presenting to gynaecologists with subjectively heavy periods are actually losing more than 80 mL per cycle, while 70% have objectively normal blood loss despite significant subjective bother.

Haemostasis in Menstrual Bleeding

Menstrual bleeding is controlled by a tightly regulated haemostatic process involving vasoconstriction of spiral arterioles, platelet plug formation, fibrin clot generation, and fibrinolysis. Prostaglandins — particularly thromboxane A2 (vasoconstriction) and prostacyclin (vasodilation) — play a central role in regulating the balance between bleeding and clotting at the endometrial surface. Matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs) facilitate tissue breakdown during menstruation, while tissue inhibitors of metalloproteinases (TIMPs) limit excessive degradation.

The local endometrial fibrinolytic system is uniquely active during menstruation. Tissue plasminogen activator (tPA) concentrations in menstrual fluid are substantially higher than in plasma, facilitating dissolution of clots within the uterine cavity to enable blood egress. Women with heavy menstrual bleeding show markedly elevated endometrial tPA activity compared to women with normal blood loss, suggesting that dysregulated fibrinolysis is a central mechanism in many cases of menorrhagia.

Hormonal Control of Menstruation

The hypothalamic–pituitary–ovarian (HPO) axis orchestrates the hormonal changes that drive the menstrual cycle. Gonadotrophin-releasing hormone (GnRH) from the hypothalamus stimulates pituitary release of follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH) and luteinising hormone (LH), which in turn regulate ovarian production of oestrogen and progesterone.

Oestrogen and Progesterone Balance

The balance between oestrogen and progesterone is fundamental to normal endometrial regulation. Oestrogen promotes endometrial growth and upregulates progesterone receptors, while progesterone opposes oestrogen's proliferative effects and prepares the endometrium for shedding. Relative or absolute oestrogen excess — whether from anovulatory cycles, obesity, or exogenous sources — leads to unopposed endometrial proliferation and heavy, often irregular, breakthrough bleeding. Conversely, inadequate progesterone production in the luteal phase results in insufficient endometrial stabilisation and heavier menstrual loss.

Prostaglandin Dysregulation

Women with heavy menstrual bleeding show altered prostaglandin profiles in the endometrium, with increased production of vasodilatory prostaglandin E2 (PGE2) and prostacyclin (PGI2) relative to vasoconstrictive thromboxane A2 (TXA2). This imbalance promotes vasodilation of spiral arterioles, inhibits platelet aggregation, and contributes to increased blood flow. Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) reduce menstrual blood loss precisely by inhibiting prostaglandin synthesis via cyclooxygenase (COX) enzyme blockade.

Definition, Classification, and Epidemiology

Modern Classification: The PALM-COEIN System

The International Federation of Gynaecology and Obstetrics (FIGO) introduced the PALM-COEIN classification system in 2011 to standardise the terminology and aetiology of abnormal uterine bleeding (AUB), replacing the older and imprecise term "dysfunctional uterine bleeding."

The PALM-COEIN Classification of AUB

P — Polyp (AUB-P): Endometrial or endocervical polyps causing irregular or heavy bleeding
A — Adenomyosis (AUB-A): Endometrial glands within the myometrium causing heavy, painful periods
L — Leiomyoma/Fibroids (AUB-L): Uterine fibroids, particularly submucosal, distorting the endometrial cavity
M — Malignancy/Hyperplasia (AUB-M): Endometrial hyperplasia or carcinoma; must always be excluded
C — Coagulopathy (AUB-C): Systemic bleeding disorders including von Willebrand disease
O — Ovulatory dysfunction (AUB-O): Anovulation or irregular ovulation causing hormonal imbalance
E — Endometrial (AUB-E): Primary endometrial disorders including fibrinolysis dysregulation
I — Iatrogenic (AUB-I): Medication-related bleeding including anticoagulants and hormonal therapies
N — Not yet classified (AUB-N): Rare or incompletely characterised causes

Practical Definition

While the historical objective definition of menorrhagia is blood loss exceeding 80 mL per cycle, contemporary clinical guidelines — including those from the UK's National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) — define heavy menstrual bleeding as "excessive menstrual blood loss which interferes with the woman's physical, emotional, social, and material quality of life, and which can occur alone or in combination with other symptoms." This patient-centred definition recognises that subjective bother and impact on daily functioning are clinically more relevant than objective volume measurements, which are rarely practical outside research settings.

Population studies estimate that heavy menstrual bleeding affects 10–30% of women of reproductive age globally. In the United Kingdom, it accounts for approximately 20% of all gynaecology outpatient referrals and is the leading indication for hysterectomy. In Australia, heavy menstrual bleeding affects approximately 1.5 million women, with annual direct healthcare costs estimated at over AUD 600 million. In the United States, AUB costs an estimated USD 1–2 billion annually in direct medical expenditure, with indirect costs (lost productivity, absenteeism, presenteeism) substantially higher.

Epidemiological Patterns

Heavy menstrual bleeding can affect women throughout the reproductive years but shows distinct epidemiological patterns across different age groups and populations.

Age-Related Patterns

Adolescents frequently experience heavy periods in the first 2–3 years after menarche due to anovulatory cycles associated with HPO axis immaturity. In this age group, coagulation disorders — particularly von Willebrand disease — are importantly prevalent and should always be considered. Women in their 30s and 40s commonly develop heavy periods secondary to uterine fibroids, adenomyosis, or endometrial polyps, with structural pathology becoming increasingly prevalent with advancing reproductive age. Perimenopause is associated with anovulatory cycles and oestrogen fluctuations that frequently cause heavy, irregular bleeding.

Impact on Quality of Life

The impact of heavy menstrual bleeding on quality of life is substantial and frequently underestimated by clinicians. Studies using validated quality-of-life instruments demonstrate that women with heavy bleeding report impaired physical functioning, reduced work and social participation, relationship difficulties, and significant psychological distress including anxiety and depression. Women with menorrhagia report missing an average of 3.6 days of normal activity per menstrual cycle, and many describe the condition as severely limiting their lives, yet many delay seeking treatment for years due to normalisation of their symptoms or lack of awareness that effective treatments exist.

Structural Causes of Heavy Menstrual Bleeding

Uterine Fibroids (Leiomyomata)

Uterine fibroids are benign smooth muscle tumours of the myometrium and represent the most common pelvic tumour in women, affecting up to 70–80% of women by age 50. However, only a subset of fibroids cause heavy menstrual bleeding, with location within the uterus being the primary determinant of symptom severity.

Fibroid Classification and Bleeding Mechanism

The FIGO classification system describes fibroid location using a numbered system from 0 to 8 based on relationship to the endometrial cavity and uterine serosa. Submucosal fibroids (types 0, 1, 2) — those within or immediately adjacent to the endometrial cavity — are most strongly associated with heavy menstrual bleeding, even when small. Intramural fibroids (types 3, 4, 5) cause heavy bleeding when they distort the endometrial cavity. Subserosal fibroids (types 6, 7) generally do not cause abnormal bleeding.

Submucosal fibroids increase menstrual blood loss by 40–100% compared to women without fibroids, even when fibroids are small (<3 cm). The mechanisms through which fibroids cause heavy bleeding include increased endometrial surface area with greater vascular exposure, impaired uterine contractility preventing normal haemostatic vasoconstriction, altered local prostaglandin and growth factor signalling, abnormal vasculogenesis with tortuous and fragile blood vessels, and dysregulated fibrinolysis. Intramural fibroids exceeding 3 cm in diameter have been shown to double the risk of heavy menstrual bleeding compared to women without fibroids.

Fibroid Growth Drivers

Fibroid growth is driven primarily by oestrogen and progesterone, explaining their emergence during the reproductive years and regression after menopause. Fibroids overexpress oestrogen receptors and aromatase (enabling local oestrogen synthesis) and exhibit enhanced progesterone receptor signalling. Somatic mutations in the gene encoding mediator complex subunit 12 (MED12) are identified in 70–85% of fibroids, representing the most common genetic alteration. Other recurrent mutations include those in HMGA1, HMGA2, and FH (fumarate hydratase). Racial disparities are striking: Black women are 2–3 times more likely to develop fibroids, have more numerous and larger fibroids at diagnosis, experience more severe symptoms, and are more likely to require surgical intervention than white women.

Adenomyosis

Adenomyosis is defined by the presence of endometrial glands and stroma within the myometrium, typically accompanied by smooth muscle hyperplasia. It is estimated to affect 20–35% of women of reproductive age, though prevalence varies widely depending on diagnostic criteria and the modality used.

Pathophysiology of Adenomyosis-Related Bleeding

The mechanisms by which adenomyosis causes heavy menstrual bleeding are multifactorial. Ectopic endometrial tissue within the myometrium bleeds in response to the same hormonal cues as the eutopic endometrium, but drainage is impaired. Additionally, adenomyosis disrupts normal uterine contractility — which is essential for haemostasis — through architectural disruption of the junctional zone myometrium and altered prostaglandin signalling. Adenomyotic lesions are associated with increased local oestrogen synthesis via upregulated aromatase activity, creating a self-perpetuating cycle of oestrogenic stimulation. Women with adenomyosis often describe not only heavy bleeding but also severe dysmenorrhoea, chronic pelvic pain, and a bulky, tender uterus.

Until recently, definitive diagnosis of adenomyosis required histological examination of hysterectomy specimens. Advances in pelvic MRI and transvaginal ultrasound (TVUS) have enabled non-invasive diagnosis with acceptable sensitivity and specificity. The uterine junctional zone — the inner myometrial layer — appears thickened (>12 mm on MRI) in adenomyosis, while TVUS may demonstrate myometrial cysts, asymmetric uterine wall thickening, and a heterogeneous myometrial echotexture. Recognition that adenomyosis can be diagnosed non-invasively and treated without hysterectomy has transformed management approaches for this condition.

Endometrial Polyps

Endometrial polyps are focal overgrowths of endometrial glands and stroma projecting into the uterine cavity. They are found in 7–10% of the general female population and in up to 25–35% of women presenting with abnormal uterine bleeding.

Polyp Biology and Clinical Significance

Endometrial polyps arise from localised oestrogen-driven endometrial proliferation in the context of reduced sensitivity to progesterone-mediated apoptosis. They express higher levels of oestrogen receptors and lower levels of progesterone receptors than surrounding endometrium, explaining their persistence throughout the menstrual cycle. Most polyps are benign, but endometrial hyperplasia and carcinoma can occur within polyps, with malignant transformation estimated at 0.5–3% of postmenopausal polyps. Polyps cause heavy or irregular bleeding through increased endometrial surface area, ulceration, and altered local haemostatic signalling. Hysteroscopic polypectomy is both diagnostic and therapeutic and is associated with symptom resolution in the majority of cases.

Endometrial Hyperplasia and Malignancy

While benign conditions account for the vast majority of heavy menstrual bleeding, endometrial hyperplasia and carcinoma must always be considered and excluded — particularly in women over 45, those with risk factors for endometrial cancer, and those with postmenopausal bleeding.

Endometrial Hyperplasia

Endometrial hyperplasia results from prolonged unopposed oestrogen stimulation without adequate progesterone opposition. The WHO 2014 classification divides hyperplasia into hyperplasia without atypia (risk of progression to carcinoma approximately 1–3%) and atypical hyperplasia/endometrioid intraepithelial neoplasia (risk of progression or concurrent carcinoma 30–50%). Risk factors include obesity (adipose tissue produces oestrogen via aromatase), polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS), anovulatory cycles, oestrogen-only hormone replacement therapy, tamoxifen use, and oestrogen-producing ovarian tumours.

Endometrial carcinoma is the most common gynaecological malignancy in high-income countries, with approximately 13,000 new cases diagnosed annually in the UK and 65,000 in the United States. It typically presents with abnormal uterine bleeding — postmenopausal bleeding in 90% of cases — which fortunately enables relatively early diagnosis. Over 75% of cases are diagnosed at stage I (confined to the uterus), with an associated 5-year survival rate of 90–95%. Women presenting with AUB should be assessed for risk of endometrial pathology, with endometrial biopsy or hysteroscopy performed when clinically indicated.

Systemic and Hormonal Causes

Coagulation Disorders

Inherited and acquired bleeding disorders are underrecognised causes of heavy menstrual bleeding that are particularly important to identify in adolescents and young women presenting with severe menorrhagia.

Von Willebrand Disease

Von Willebrand disease (VWD) is the most common inherited bleeding disorder, affecting approximately 1% of the population. It results from deficiency or dysfunction of von Willebrand factor (VWF), a multimeric glycoprotein essential for platelet adhesion and as a carrier protein for clotting factor VIII. Heavy menstrual bleeding is the most common presenting symptom in women with VWD, with 74–92% of affected women reporting menorrhagia. Studies show that up to 13–20% of women presenting with heavy menstrual bleeding have an underlying coagulation disorder, with VWD accounting for the majority. This figure rises to approximately 36% in adolescents hospitalised for severe menorrhagia, underscoring the importance of haematological evaluation in this age group.

Other Coagulation Disorders

Platelet function disorders — including platelet storage pool disease, Glanzmann's thrombasthenia, and Bernard-Soulier syndrome — can cause significant menorrhagia. Factor XI deficiency is more common in Ashkenazi Jewish populations and frequently manifests with heavy menstrual bleeding. Acquired coagulopathies from anticoagulant medications (warfarin, direct oral anticoagulants), severe liver disease, or thrombocytopenia are important iatrogenic or systemic causes. Women beginning anticoagulation therapy should be counselled about the risk of heavy menstrual bleeding and offered proactive treatment strategies.

Ovulatory Dysfunction

Anovulation or irregular ovulation results in absent or inadequate progesterone production, leading to unopposed oestrogen stimulation of the endometrium. This produces a proliferating but unstable endometrium that eventually breaks down in an unpredictable and often heavy fashion.

Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS)

PCOS is the most common cause of anovulatory AUB in women of reproductive age, affecting 8–13% of women globally. The hallmarks of PCOS — hyperandrogenism, ovulatory dysfunction, and polycystic ovarian morphology on ultrasound — are underpinned by insulin resistance, hyperinsulinaemia, and dysregulated LH secretion. Chronic anovulation leads to continuous oestrogen exposure without progesterone opposition, causing endometrial hyperplasia and heavy, irregular bleeding. Women with PCOS carry a three-fold increased lifetime risk of endometrial cancer due to chronic unopposed oestrogen stimulation, making endometrial protection a key management consideration.

Thyroid Disorders

Both hypothyroidism and hyperthyroidism can cause menstrual irregularities and heavy bleeding. Hypothyroidism — inadequate thyroid hormone production — affects menstruation through multiple mechanisms including reduced sex hormone-binding globulin (SHBG) production (elevating free oestrogen), impaired ovulation, reduced factor VIII and VWF levels, and decreased platelet aggregation. Thyroid function tests should be performed in all women presenting with heavy menstrual bleeding, as treatment of the underlying thyroid disorder frequently resolves or substantially improves menstrual symptoms without specific gynaecological intervention.

Hypothyroidism is identified in 2–5% of women presenting with heavy menstrual bleeding in primary care settings, rising to 10–15% in studies specifically screening populations with menstrual complaints. The association between subclinical hypothyroidism (elevated TSH with normal free T4) and menstrual irregularity is less consistent. Hyperprolactinaemia — from pituitary microadenoma, hypothyroidism, or medication — can suppress ovulation and cause irregular, often light periods, but occasionally presents with heavy bleeding in the context of oestrogen-driven endometrial proliferation during anovulatory cycles.

Iatrogenic Causes

Several medications and medical devices can cause or exacerbate heavy menstrual bleeding, and a thorough medication history is essential in the assessment of any woman presenting with AUB.

Anticoagulants and Antiplatelet Agents

Anticoagulant therapy — including warfarin, heparin, and direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs) such as rivaroxaban, apixaban, and dabigatran — significantly increases menstrual blood loss. Studies report that 22–65% of women on DOACs experience heavy menstrual bleeding, often representing a major quality-of-life burden. Antiplatelet agents including aspirin and clopidogrel can also worsen menstrual bleeding, though generally to a lesser degree than anticoagulants.

Copper Intrauterine Devices

Copper intrauterine devices (Cu-IUDs) are a highly effective non-hormonal contraceptive but are associated with a 20–50% increase in menstrual blood loss, particularly in the first 3–6 months after insertion. The mechanism involves a local inflammatory response to copper ions that alters prostaglandin signalling, increases fibrinolytic activity, and impairs platelet function within the endometrium. Women who already have heavy periods may find the Cu-IUD significantly worsens their symptoms, and this should be discussed during contraceptive counselling.

Impact on Systemic Health: Iron Deficiency and Anaemia

Iron Deficiency Anaemia as a Consequence of Heavy Periods

Heavy menstrual bleeding is the leading cause of iron deficiency anaemia (IDA) in premenopausal women in high-income countries, and its systemic consequences extend well beyond fatigue.

Pathophysiology of Menstrual Iron Loss

Menstrual blood loss of 80 mL equates to iron loss of approximately 40 mg per cycle — equivalent to the amount typically absorbed from the diet over 20 days. Women with menorrhagia losing 200 mL per cycle lose approximately 100 mg of iron per menstruation, vastly exceeding typical dietary iron absorption capacity (1–2 mg/day in most women). This creates a progressive negative iron balance that first depletes iron stores (ferritin falls), then reduces iron supply to the bone marrow (transferrin saturation falls, soluble transferrin receptor rises), and ultimately impairs haemoglobin synthesis, producing a microcytic, hypochromic anaemia.

Iron deficiency — even without frank anaemia — has clinically significant consequences that are frequently overlooked. Iron is essential not only for haemoglobin synthesis but also for mitochondrial energy production, thyroid hormone synthesis, neurotransmitter production (dopamine, serotonin), and immune function. Symptoms of iron deficiency include fatigue, impaired exercise tolerance, brain fog, poor concentration, restless legs syndrome, hair loss, pica (cravings for non-food items such as ice), and impaired immune function. These symptoms profoundly affect quality of life and are often dismissed or attributed to other causes before the underlying haematological explanation is identified.
A 2023 analysis of Australian primary care data found that approximately 35% of women aged 18–49 presenting with heavy menstrual bleeding had serum ferritin below 30 µg/L (indicating depleted iron stores), and 12% had frank iron deficiency anaemia (haemoglobin <120 g/L with microcytosis). Women with heavy periods and iron deficiency report significantly worse fatigue and cognitive function scores compared to women with heavy periods but normal iron stores, confirming the additive burden of IDA. Despite this, iron studies are not universally performed during assessment of heavy menstrual bleeding in primary care.

Diagnosis and Treatment of Iron Deficiency

Serum ferritin is the most sensitive single marker of iron stores, though it is an acute-phase reactant that can be falsely elevated in the context of inflammation or infection. A ferritin below 30 µg/L reliably indicates depleted iron stores in the absence of inflammation; some haematologists advocate for a threshold of 50 µg/L in women with symptoms of iron deficiency. Oral iron supplementation — typically ferrous sulfate 200 mg once or twice daily — remains first-line therapy for IDA, though gastrointestinal side effects limit tolerability in many patients. Intravenous iron formulations (ferric carboxymaltose, iron sucrose) provide rapid iron repletion with excellent tolerability and are increasingly used for women with significant IDA, intolerance to oral iron, or malabsorption.

Diagnosis and Clinical Assessment

History and Clinical Evaluation

A structured clinical history is the foundation of assessment for heavy menstrual bleeding, enabling characterisation of bleeding patterns, identification of potential causes, and assessment of impact on quality of life.

Key Elements of the Menstrual History

Assessment should include cycle length (typically 21–35 days), duration of bleeding (typically 3–7 days), subjective assessment of flow (number of pads or tampons used per day, presence of clots, frequency of flooding or soiling clothing or bedding), and associated symptoms such as dysmenorrhoea, intermenstrual bleeding, postcoital bleeding, and pelvic pressure or pain. Duration and severity of symptoms, prior investigations and treatments, obstetric history, and family history of bleeding disorders or gynaecological conditions are all relevant. The pictorial blood assessment chart (PBAC) is a validated semi-quantitative tool that uses pictorial representations of pad and tampon saturation to estimate blood loss; scores above 100 correlate with blood loss exceeding 80 mL per cycle with reasonable accuracy.

Screening for Coagulopathy

A structured bleeding history should be taken in all women presenting with heavy menstrual bleeding, particularly in adolescents and young women. A validated screening tool for VWD in women with menorrhagia asks about: heavy menstrual bleeding since menarche; family history of bleeding disorder; personal history of postpartum haemorrhage, surgical or dental bleeding, or bruising. A positive screen should prompt referral for formal haematological evaluation including VWF antigen, VWF activity (ristocetin cofactor assay), and factor VIII levels.

Investigations

Investigation of heavy menstrual bleeding should be guided by the clinical history and examination findings, with the dual aims of identifying underlying pathology and assessing the systemic consequences of bleeding.

Recommended Investigations for Heavy Menstrual Bleeding

Full blood count (FBC): To identify anaemia (haemoglobin, MCV, MCH)
Serum ferritin: Essential to detect iron deficiency even without anaemia
Thyroid function tests (TFTs): TSH as minimum; free T4 if TSH abnormal
Transvaginal ultrasound (TVUS): First-line imaging to detect fibroids, adenomyosis, polyps
Endometrial biopsy (pipelle): Indicated in women ≥45, risk factors for endometrial cancer, persistent AUB not responding to treatment
Hysteroscopy: Direct visualisation of uterine cavity; gold standard for polyp and fibroid detection; enables simultaneous treatment
Pelvic MRI: For precise characterisation of fibroid number, size, and location; adenomyosis diagnosis
Coagulation screen: VWF antigen/activity, factor VIII, in adolescents and those with suggestive history
Pregnancy test: To exclude pregnancy-related bleeding in women of reproductive age

Transvaginal Ultrasound

Transvaginal ultrasound (TVUS) is the first-line imaging investigation for women with heavy menstrual bleeding and is superior to transabdominal ultrasound for endometrial and myometrial assessment. It enables detection of fibroids (intracavitary, intramural, subserosal), endometrial polyps (with or without saline infusion sonohysterography), adenomyosis (junctional zone thickening, myometrial cysts, asymmetric wall thickness), and endometrial thickness measurement. Saline infusion sonohysterography (SIS) — in which sterile saline is instilled into the uterine cavity under ultrasound guidance — significantly improves detection of intrauterine lesions such as polyps and submucosal fibroids, with sensitivity and specificity approaching that of hysteroscopy.

Hysteroscopy

Hysteroscopy — direct visualisation of the uterine cavity using a thin telescope — is the gold standard investigation for intrauterine pathology and enables simultaneous diagnosis and treatment. Office hysteroscopy using modern miniaturised hysteroscopes (2–5 mm diameter) can be performed without anaesthesia in most women, enabling polypectomy, targeted biopsy, and small fibroid resection as outpatient procedures. Operative hysteroscopy in a theatre setting enables resection of larger submucosal fibroids (TCRM) and endometrial ablation. Hysteroscopy has a sensitivity of 80–90% and specificity of 90–95% for detection of intrauterine pathology.

Medical Treatments

Hormonal Therapies

Hormonal treatments represent the cornerstone of medical management for heavy menstrual bleeding, working through multiple mechanisms to reduce endometrial proliferation, inhibit prostaglandin synthesis, and suppress the menstrual cycle.

Levonorgestrel Intrauterine System (LNG-IUS)

The levonorgestrel intrauterine system (LNG-IUS, marketed as Mirena) is the most effective medical treatment for heavy menstrual bleeding and is recommended as first-line hormonal therapy by most international guidelines, including NICE, RANZCOG, and ACOG. It delivers levonorgestrel (a synthetic progestogen) directly to the endometrium at a dose of 20 µg/day, causing profound endometrial suppression, decidualisation, and ultimately endometrial atrophy while maintaining ovarian function and systemic hormone levels.

Randomised controlled trials demonstrate that the LNG-IUS reduces menstrual blood loss by 74–97% at 12 months, with up to 40% of women achieving amenorrhoea (cessation of periods). The ECLIPSE trial — a large pragmatic UK randomised trial — found that the LNG-IUS was more effective than usual medical treatment (including combined oral contraceptives, NSAIDs, and tranexamic acid) in reducing menstrual bleeding and improving quality of life at 5 years, and was associated with significantly lower rates of surgical intervention. The LNG-IUS also provides highly effective contraception for 5–8 years and offers protection against endometrial hyperplasia in women on oestrogen therapy.

Combined Oral Contraceptive Pill

The combined oral contraceptive pill (COCP) reduces menstrual blood loss by 40–65% through endometrial suppression, inhibition of ovulation, and altered prostaglandin synthesis. It is particularly appropriate for women who desire contraception alongside management of heavy menstrual bleeding. The COCP also reduces dysmenorrhoea, regulates the cycle, and may reduce fibroid and ovarian cyst formation with long-term use. Absolute contraindications include current or past venous thromboembolism, migraine with aura, cardiovascular disease, hypertension, and active liver disease — a thorough risk assessment is essential before prescription.

Oral Progestogen Therapy

Cyclic oral norethisterone (5–15 mg daily from days 5–26 of the cycle) can reduce heavy menstrual bleeding and regulate the cycle through endometrial stabilisation and suppression. However, systemic side effects including bloating, mood changes, acne, and weight gain are common and limit acceptability. Continuous oral progestogen is used for treatment of endometrial hyperplasia and for management of heavy bleeding in the context of anovulation. The Depo-Provera injectable medroxyprogesterone acetate causes amenorrhoea in approximately 50% of users by 12 months through hypothalamic suppression but takes several months to establish cycle control, and irregular bleeding is common in the initial months.

GnRH Analogues and Antagonists

GnRH agonists (leuprorelin, goserelin, nafarelin) cause initial gonadotrophin stimulation (flare) followed by profound downregulation of the HPO axis, producing a reversible menopausal state with amenorrhoea in the majority of women. They are highly effective for reducing heavy menstrual bleeding and fibroid volume (20–40% reduction in uterine volume) but are generally limited to 3–6 months of use due to hypo-oestrogenic side effects including hot flushes, vaginal dryness, and bone mineral density loss. GnRH antagonists (elagolix, relugolix, linzagolix) achieve rapid, competitive HPO suppression without the initial flare and can be used with add-back oestrogen/progestogen therapy to enable longer-term use. Relugolix combined with oestradiol/norethindrone acetate add-back (Ryeqo) is now licensed for uterine fibroid-associated heavy menstrual bleeding and enables sustained bleeding control with acceptable hormonal side effects.

Non-Hormonal Medical Treatments

Non-hormonal treatments are important options for women who cannot or prefer not to use hormonal therapies, and include agents targeting prostaglandin synthesis and fibrinolysis.

Tranexamic Acid

Tranexamic acid is an antifibrinolytic agent that inhibits the activation of plasminogen to plasmin, thereby reducing fibrinolytic activity within the endometrium and decreasing menstrual blood loss. It is taken only during menstruation (typically 1–1.5 g three times daily for up to 5 days per cycle) and has no effect on hormonal balance or ovulation. Multiple randomised trials demonstrate 30–58% reduction in menstrual blood loss with tranexamic acid, with a meta-analysis confirming superior efficacy to NSAIDs and comparable efficacy to combined oral contraceptives as a non-hormonal option.

The largest systematic review of tranexamic acid for heavy menstrual bleeding, incorporating data from 13 randomised controlled trials involving over 1,000 women, found a mean reduction in menstrual blood loss of 53% compared to placebo (weighted mean difference −94 mL; 95% CI −116 to −71 mL). Quality-of-life measures including work and social activities were significantly improved. Thromboembolic events — a theoretical concern with antifibrinolytic therapy — were not increased in clinical trials at doses used for menorrhagia, though the drug should be used with caution in women with thrombotic risk factors.

Non-Steroidal Anti-Inflammatory Drugs (NSAIDs)

NSAIDs reduce menstrual blood loss by 25–35% through inhibition of cyclooxygenase (COX) enzymes, reducing prostaglandin synthesis and thereby improving the balance between vasoconstrictive and vasodilatory prostanoids. They are particularly effective when heavy bleeding is accompanied by dysmenorrhoea, as they address both symptoms simultaneously. Mefenamic acid (500 mg three times daily) and ibuprofen (400–800 mg three times daily) are most commonly used, taken during menstruation. NSAIDs are less effective than tranexamic acid or hormonal treatments for reducing blood loss but are widely available, inexpensive, and familiar to most patients. Gastrointestinal side effects limit use in some women, particularly those with peptic ulcer disease or renal impairment.

Surgical Treatments

Endometrial Ablation

Endometrial ablation destroys or removes the endometrial lining of the uterus, producing amenorrhoea or significantly reduced menstrual flow. It is indicated for women with heavy menstrual bleeding who have completed their family, have no structural pathology requiring removal, and prefer surgical treatment over long-term medical management.

Second-Generation Ablation Techniques

Second-generation "global" ablation devices have largely replaced first-generation hysteroscopic techniques (rollerball, TCRE loop resection) due to their shorter operating time, ability to be performed under local or light anaesthesia, and similar efficacy. These devices use various energy sources to achieve global endometrial destruction:

Thermal balloon ablation (Thermachoice, Cavaterm) circulates heated fluid within a balloon placed in the uterine cavity, achieving endometrial ablation through thermal injury. Microwave endometrial ablation (MEA) uses microwave energy to heat and destroy endometrial tissue. Radiofrequency ablation (NovaSure) uses bipolar radiofrequency energy delivered via a mesh electrode to ablate the endometrium. Cryoablation (Her Option) uses a cryoprobe to freeze and destroy the endometrium. Hydrothermal ablation (Hydro ThermAblator) circulates heated fluid freely throughout the uterine cavity.

Meta-analyses of second-generation endometrial ablation demonstrate amenorrhoea rates of 30–60% and patient satisfaction rates of 80–90% at 2 years. At 5 years, approximately 20–25% of women who have undergone endometrial ablation will have required further surgical intervention, most commonly hysterectomy, due to recurrent bleeding or treatment failure. NovaSure radiofrequency ablation achieves the highest amenorrhoea rates of currently available second-generation techniques (41–56% amenorrhoea at 12 months in randomised trials). Contraindications include desire for future fertility, known or suspected endometrial cancer or hyperplasia, active pelvic infection, and certain uterine anomalies.

Long-Term Outcomes and Post-Ablation Syndrome

Post-ablation tubal sterilisation syndrome — characterised by cyclic pelvic pain developing months to years after ablation — can occur when residual endometrial tissue bleeds but drainage is obstructed due to synechiae (scar tissue). This complication, occurring in approximately 2–8% of women with prior tubal sterilisation who undergo endometrial ablation, underscores the importance of careful patient selection. Women with adenomyosis have lower success rates following endometrial ablation, with higher rates of ongoing pain and reoperation, as ablation does not address ectopic endometrial tissue within the myometrium.

Hysteroscopic Myomectomy and Polypectomy

Hysteroscopic surgery enables removal of intrauterine pathology causing heavy menstrual bleeding while preserving the uterus — making it suitable for women who wish to retain fertility or avoid hysterectomy.

Hysteroscopic Fibroid Resection (TCRM)

Transcervical resection of fibroid (TCRM) using an operative hysteroscope can remove submucosal fibroids (FIGO types 0, 1, and selected type 2) causing heavy menstrual bleeding. Complete resection of submucosal fibroids is achieved in 80–95% of type 0 and type 1 fibroids and leads to significant improvement in menstrual blood loss in 70–85% of cases. Fibroid size, location, and degree of myometrial extension influence technical success and the need for staged procedures. The morcellator-based TRUCLEAR and MyoSure systems enable rapid fibroid removal in the office or theatre setting without the risk of fluid absorption associated with monopolar hysteroscopic electrosurgery.

Uterine Fibroid Embolisation

Uterine fibroid embolisation (UFE, also called uterine artery embolisation — UAE) is an interventional radiology procedure that occludes the uterine arteries supplying fibroids, causing ischaemic necrosis and fibroid shrinkage while preserving the uterus.

UFE Procedure and Outcomes

UFE is performed under fluoroscopic guidance via a femoral or radial arterial approach. Polyvinyl alcohol particles or microspheres are injected bilaterally into the uterine arteries to obstruct fibroid blood supply. Fibroids undergo ischaemic necrosis over 3–6 months, typically shrinking by 40–60% in volume. Menstrual blood loss is reduced in approximately 85–90% of patients. The procedure is performed under conscious sedation with overnight hospitalisation for pain management; post-embolisation syndrome (fever, pain, nausea) due to fibroid ischaemia occurs in the majority of patients but is self-limiting. Long-term patient satisfaction and clinical success rates of 80–85% at 5 years compare favourably with hysterectomy in terms of quality of life, though reoperation rates are higher than with hysterectomy.

The REST trial — a UK randomised trial comparing UFE with hysterectomy — found similar health-related quality-of-life scores at 1 year, with UFE associated with shorter hospital stay and faster return to normal activities. However, UFE had significantly higher rates of additional intervention (surgical or repeat embolisation) at 5 years compared to hysterectomy (32% vs 0%). Data regarding the impact of UFE on subsequent fertility are limited, but pregnancy is not precluded by the procedure, and a number of successful pregnancies have been reported following UFE. UFE is generally preferred over myomectomy or hysterectomy in women with multiple or large fibroids who wish to preserve their uterus and avoid major surgery.

Focused Ultrasound and Radiofrequency Ablation of Fibroids

Minimally invasive approaches to fibroid treatment include magnetic resonance-guided focused ultrasound (MRgFUS) and transcervical or laparoscopic radiofrequency fibroid ablation, offering uterus-preserving treatment with minimal procedural invasiveness.

MR-Guided Focused Ultrasound (MRgFUS)

MRgFUS uses concentrated ultrasound waves guided by real-time MRI imaging to ablate fibroid tissue through thermal coagulative necrosis, without incisions or radiation. It is performed as a day procedure without general anaesthesia. Clinical trials demonstrate fibroid volume reductions of 15–40% and significant symptom improvement in appropriately selected patients. However, eligibility criteria are restrictive (fibroid location, size, number, and MRI characteristics), treatment times are long (2–4 hours per session), reintervention rates are higher than with UFE or surgery, and not all patients achieve durable symptom relief. MRgFUS is most suitable for women with a limited number of accessible, well-vascularised fibroids who wish to avoid surgery or UFE.

Myomectomy

Myomectomy — surgical removal of fibroids while preserving the uterus — is the standard surgical treatment for symptomatic fibroids in women who wish to retain fertility or avoid hysterectomy.

Laparoscopic, Robotic, and Open Myomectomy

Myomectomy can be performed laparoscopically, with robotic assistance, or via open laparotomy depending on fibroid number, size, and location. Laparoscopic myomectomy is appropriate for most intramural and subserosal fibroids and is associated with shorter hospital stay, reduced blood loss, and faster recovery compared to open surgery. Robotic myomectomy offers technical advantages for complex procedures but adds cost without proven superior outcomes over standard laparoscopy in most studies. Open myomectomy (laparotomy) remains preferable for women with very large uteri, numerous fibroids, or complex anatomy. Fibroid recurrence rates after myomectomy are 15–30% at 5 years, and up to 15–20% of women require additional surgical intervention within 5–10 years.

Hysterectomy

Hysterectomy — removal of the uterus — is the definitive surgical treatment for heavy menstrual bleeding and provides a permanent cure. It is appropriate for women who have completed their family, in whom medical treatment has failed or is contraindicated, or who prefer definitive surgical management.

Approaches to Hysterectomy

Hysterectomy can be performed vaginally, laparoscopically (total laparoscopic hysterectomy, TLH), with robotic assistance, or via laparotomy. Vaginal and laparoscopic approaches are preferred over laparotomy when technically feasible, as they are associated with shorter hospital stay, less blood loss, fewer complications, and faster recovery. Total hysterectomy (removal of uterus and cervix) is generally preferred over subtotal (supracervical) hysterectomy to eliminate cervical cancer risk. Bilateral salpingo-oophorectomy at the time of hysterectomy is a decision that must be individualised, balancing the risk reduction for ovarian cancer against the consequences of surgical menopause, particularly in women under 50.

In Australia and the UK, hysterectomy rates for menorrhagia have declined substantially over the past two decades — by approximately 50–60% — following widespread adoption of the LNG-IUS and endometrial ablation. Despite this, hysterectomy remains the second most common major gynaecological procedure after Caesarean section in high-income countries. Patient satisfaction after hysterectomy for heavy menstrual bleeding is very high, consistently exceeding 90% in long-term follow-up studies, with significant improvements in quality of life, sexual function, and psychological wellbeing reported by the majority of women.

Special Populations and Considerations

Heavy Periods in Adolescents

Heavy menstrual bleeding in adolescents requires a distinct clinical approach, with important differences in aetiology, investigation, and management compared to adult women.

Anovulatory Cycles in Adolescence

The HPO axis typically requires 2–3 years to mature fully after menarche, and anovulatory cycles — and the heavy, irregular bleeding they cause — are physiologically normal in this period. However, anovulatory bleeding that is sufficiently heavy to cause anaemia, requires hospitalisation, or significantly impacts school attendance and quality of life warrants investigation and treatment. The threshold for investigation should be lower in adolescents than in adult women, as the prevalence of underlying coagulation disorders (particularly VWD) is substantially higher in this age group.

Management in Adolescents

The combined oral contraceptive pill is frequently used as first-line treatment in adolescents with anovulatory heavy menstrual bleeding due to its cycle-regulating and bleeding-reducing effects, with the additional benefit of contraception as required. The LNG-IUS is increasingly used in adolescents as the evidence for its safety, tolerability, and efficacy in this age group has grown. Iron supplementation is essential in anaemic adolescents. In adolescents with confirmed coagulation disorders, treatment should be planned collaboratively between gynaecology and haematology; tranexamic acid, desmopressin (for VWD), and factor replacement therapies may be appropriate depending on the specific disorder.

Heavy Periods and Fertility

When heavy menstrual bleeding is associated with structural pathology — particularly fibroids and endometrial polyps — treatment may improve fertility outcomes as well as menstrual symptoms.

Fibroids and Fertility

Submucosal fibroids significantly impair implantation and clinical pregnancy rates, with systematic reviews demonstrating a 50–70% reduction in live birth rates compared to infertile women without fibroids. Hysteroscopic myomectomy for submucosal fibroids substantially improves fertility outcomes, with live birth rates of 40–60% reported in IVF cycles following resection. Intramural fibroids that distort the endometrial cavity also have a negative impact on fertility, though the effect of non-cavity-distorting intramural fibroids on fertility remains debated. Women with symptomatic fibroids who wish to conceive should be referred for fertility counselling and consideration of uterus-preserving treatment.

Heavy Periods in Perimenopause

Perimenopause — the transition to menopause typically beginning in the mid-40s — is characterised by increasing cycle irregularity, anovulation, and fluctuating hormone levels that frequently manifest as heavy and unpredictable menstrual bleeding.

Distinguishing Perimenopausal AUB from Pathology

While anovulatory bleeding is physiologically common in perimenopause, it is essential to exclude structural pathology and endometrial hyperplasia or carcinoma in women presenting with AUB in this age group. Endometrial biopsy (pipelle) is recommended for all women aged 45 and over presenting with AUB, as well as in younger women with risk factors for endometrial pathology (obesity, PCOS, diabetes, family history of endometrial cancer, use of tamoxifen). The LNG-IUS is an excellent treatment option in perimenopause, providing effective bleeding control, endometrial protection against hyperplasia, and intrauterine progestogen coverage for women on oestrogen-containing hormone therapy.

The Role of Lifestyle, Nutrition, and Emerging Approaches

Diet, Body Weight, and Menstrual Blood Loss

While pharmacological and surgical treatments form the evidence base of heavy menstrual bleeding management, lifestyle and nutritional factors may influence menstrual blood loss and should be considered as adjunctive elements of a comprehensive management plan.

Obesity and Menstrual Bleeding

Obesity is associated with heavy and irregular menstrual bleeding through multiple mechanisms: increased peripheral oestrogen production via adipose tissue aromatase activity, which promotes endometrial proliferation; higher rates of anovulation and PCOS; elevated insulin levels, which stimulate ovarian androgen production and disrupt the HPO axis; and chronic low-grade inflammation affecting endometrial biology. Intentional weight loss in obese women with anovulatory AUB frequently results in spontaneous restoration of regular ovulation and normalisation of menstrual patterns, and should be supported alongside specific menstrual treatments.

Nutritional Factors

Evidence for specific dietary interventions in heavy menstrual bleeding is limited, but several nutritional factors have biological plausibility. Omega-3 fatty acids from oily fish may modulate prostaglandin balance in favour of vasoconstrictive prostanoids, potentially reducing blood loss. Vitamin D deficiency — highly prevalent in temperate populations including Australia's southern states — has been associated with fibroid development and abnormal uterine bleeding in observational studies, though intervention trials have not yet established a causal effect. Magnesium plays a role in smooth muscle relaxation and prostaglandin metabolism, and dietary magnesium deficiency may worsen menstrual cramping. Adequate dietary iron intake is important to mitigate the risk of iron deficiency in women with heavy bleeding, though supplementation is generally necessary when clinical deficiency is established.

Stress, Sleep, and Menstrual Health

Psychological stress and sleep disruption influence the HPO axis and can affect menstrual cycle regularity and bleeding patterns. Activation of the hypothalamic–pituitary–adrenal (HPA) axis under chronic stress increases cortisol secretion, which can suppress GnRH pulsatility and disrupt ovulation. Sleep deprivation has been shown to alter LH secretion patterns and reduce progesterone levels, potentially contributing to anovulatory cycles and heavy bleeding. While evidence for specific stress-reduction or sleep interventions improving menstrual blood loss is lacking, optimising sleep quality and stress management as part of general health management is reasonable and consistent with overall wellbeing.

Emerging Medical Therapies

Several novel medical therapies for heavy menstrual bleeding are in clinical development or have recently received regulatory approval, expanding the treatment armamentarium.

Oral GnRH Antagonist Combinations

Oral GnRH antagonist formulations (elagolix, relugolix, linzagolix) combined with add-back oestradiol and progestogen represent a significant advance in medical management of fibroid-associated heavy menstrual bleeding. Phase III trials of relugolix combination therapy (Ryeqo) demonstrated amenorrhoea or light bleeding in 73–74% of women at 24 weeks compared to 19% with placebo, with improvements maintained over 52 weeks and acceptable bone density effects with add-back therapy. Elagolix (Oriahnn) has been approved in the United States for up to 24 months of use for fibroid-associated heavy menstrual bleeding. These agents provide an oral, non-surgical option for women who may be awaiting surgery, prefer medical management, or are not surgical candidates.

Vilaprisan and Selective Progesterone Receptor Modulators

Selective progesterone receptor modulators (SPRMs) including ulipristal acetate (UPA) act on the progesterone receptor to suppress endometrial proliferation and reduce fibroid volume. UPA (Esmya/Fibristal) was widely used in Europe for pre-surgical fibroid treatment and long-term intermittent therapy, but was suspended in 2020 following reports of serious liver injury in a small number of patients. Research into safer SPRMs with improved hepatic profiles continues, with vilaprisan showing efficacy in phase II trials. The role of SPRMs in fibroid management may be re-established if compounds with improved safety profiles gain regulatory approval.

Psychological and Social Impact

Mental Health and Heavy Menstrual Bleeding

The psychological impact of heavy menstrual bleeding is substantial and bidirectional — mental health conditions can worsen menstrual symptoms, while severe menstrual symptoms contribute to anxiety, depression, and reduced wellbeing.

Anxiety, Depression, and Treatment Seeking

Studies consistently demonstrate elevated rates of depression and anxiety in women with heavy menstrual bleeding compared to general population controls. A 2022 systematic review found that women with heavy menstrual bleeding were 1.8 times more likely to have depression and 2.2 times more likely to have anxiety than women with normal periods. Fear of visible bleeding accidents, need for frequent bathroom visits, avoidance of social and physical activities, and chronic fatigue from iron deficiency all contribute to social isolation and reduced self-esteem. Despite this burden, studies consistently show that many women delay seeking help for years, often normalising their symptoms or underestimating the degree of impact they are experiencing. Clinicians should routinely enquire about psychosocial impact and quality of life, as these often drive treatment preferences more than objective measures of blood loss.

Work, Productivity, and Educational Impact

The economic and professional impact of heavy menstrual bleeding is substantial. Surveys of working women with heavy menstrual bleeding report that 80–90% say their condition affects their ability to work, with presenteeism (attending work while impaired) representing the largest component of productivity loss. Women report avoiding physical activity, social events, and travel during menstruation, with many describing their lives as structured around their menstrual cycle. A 2021 Australian study estimated that heavy menstrual bleeding costs the Australian economy over AUD 340 million per year in lost productivity, equivalent to 1.3 million working days per year.

Racial and Ethnic Disparities in Diagnosis and Treatment

Significant disparities exist in the diagnosis, treatment, and outcomes of conditions causing heavy menstrual bleeding across racial and ethnic groups.

Disparities in Fibroid Management

Black women have a substantially higher burden of uterine fibroids, experiencing them earlier, with greater severity, and with higher rates of anaemia and surgical intervention. Despite this higher burden, studies from the United States show that Black women are more likely to undergo hysterectomy and less likely to receive uterus-preserving treatments (myomectomy, UFE) compared to white women, even after adjusting for fibroid characteristics and patient preferences. This disparity may reflect differential access to subspecialty care, implicit bias in clinical decision-making, or differential insurance coverage for uterus-preserving procedures. Advocacy for equitable access to the full range of treatment options — particularly fertility-preserving and uterus-preserving approaches — is an important dimension of comprehensive fibroid care.

Research from the US Sister Study cohort found that Black women were 65% more likely to report heavy menstrual bleeding than white women (OR 1.65; 95% CI 1.52–1.79) after adjusting for age, BMI, and reproductive factors. This disparity is not fully explained by fibroid prevalence and likely reflects additional contributions from differences in HPO axis function, inflammatory signalling, and socioeconomic factors affecting healthcare access and stress. Studies have also found that Black women are less likely to have their menstrual symptoms taken seriously by healthcare providers, resulting in longer diagnostic delays and greater cumulative morbidity.

A Patient-Centred Approach to Heavy Menstrual Bleeding

Shared Decision-Making

Management of heavy menstrual bleeding should be guided by patient values, preferences, and priorities alongside clinical evidence. No single treatment is universally best, and the optimal approach depends on the underlying cause, severity of symptoms, desire for future fertility, contraceptive needs, comorbidities, and the patient's own treatment goals.

Effective management begins with asking women what matters most to them — whether that is reducing blood loss to a manageable level, achieving amenorrhoea, preserving fertility, avoiding surgery, or obtaining permanent resolution. Treatment goals and priorities vary enormously between women and should not be assumed. A woman who fears surgery may prioritise the LNG-IUS as a long-term medical option; a woman who has completed her family and wants definitive treatment may choose endometrial ablation or hysterectomy. Both choices are valid, and the clinician's role is to provide accurate, unbiased information about the benefits, risks, and limitations of all relevant options to support informed decision-making.

The NICE Guideline Approach

The UK's NICE guideline on heavy menstrual bleeding (NG88, updated 2023) recommends a stepped approach starting with first-line medical treatments — particularly the LNG-IUS for women not seeking immediate pregnancy — before escalating to second-line medical therapies (tranexamic acid, NSAIDs, COCPs, oral progestogens) and subsequently surgical options (endometrial ablation, hysterectomy). The guideline emphasises that women should be given balanced information about all available options and be supported to make decisions consistent with their own values and preferences. Hysterectomy should not be delayed unreasonably in women who have clearly expressed a preference for definitive surgical management after considering all alternatives.

When to Seek Medical Attention

Red Flags Requiring Urgent Assessment

Postmenopausal bleeding: Any vaginal bleeding more than 12 months after the last period requires urgent investigation to exclude endometrial cancer
Intermenstrual bleeding: Bleeding between periods may indicate endometrial pathology, cervical disease, or infection
Postcoital bleeding: Bleeding following sexual intercourse warrants cervical assessment to exclude cervical pathology
Sudden severe anaemia: Heavy bleeding causing haemoglobin below 80 g/L or symptoms of severe anaemia (breathlessness, palpitations at rest, chest pain) may require hospitalisation
New or changed symptoms: A change in previously stable menstrual pattern — particularly in women over 45 — requires reassessment
Bleeding affecting function: Bleeding interfering with work, education, sleep, or daily activities deserves evaluation regardless of absolute blood volume
Adolescent severe bleeding: Heavy bleeding in a teenager — particularly at menarche — requires screening for coagulation disorders

Practical Self-Management and Support

While Awaiting Diagnosis or Treatment

Women managing heavy periods while awaiting specialist review or while medical treatments take effect may find the following approaches helpful.

Practical Management Strategies

Over-the-counter tranexamic acid (where available) and ibuprofen can reduce blood loss without requiring a prescription in many countries. High-absorbency menstrual products including period underwear, menstrual cups, and overnight pads provide practical protection and may reduce anxiety about visible accidents. Iron-rich foods — red meat, legumes, dark leafy vegetables, fortified cereals — alongside vitamin C (which enhances non-haem iron absorption) should be prioritised in the diet. Women who develop symptoms of significant iron deficiency should proactively request iron studies rather than waiting for routine checks. Keeping a menstrual symptom diary or using a validated app to track bleeding patterns, pad use, and symptoms provides useful documentation for clinical consultations and helps women recognise patterns in their symptoms.

Advocacy in Clinical Settings

Women should feel empowered to advocate for thorough assessment of their menstrual symptoms. Heavy menstrual bleeding is a legitimate medical condition with effective treatments, not a normal part of womanhood to be endured. Seeking a second opinion, requesting investigation of iron levels, asking for referral to a gynaecologist, and asking specifically about all available treatment options — including the LNG-IUS, endometrial ablation, and uterus-preserving surgical approaches — are all appropriate and reasonable steps. Patient advocacy organisations and condition-specific resources (such as the Jean Hailes Foundation for Women's Health in Australia, or the National Heavy Flow Foundation) provide evidence-based information to support informed decision-making.

Conclusion

Heavy menstrual bleeding is a highly prevalent, often undertreated condition with a broad spectrum of causes and a significant impact on quality of life, productivity, and systemic health. Advances in understanding the molecular and structural mechanisms driving heavy bleeding have led to an expanding range of effective treatments — from the highly effective and reversible LNG-IUS to outpatient hysteroscopic surgery to novel oral GnRH antagonist therapies. No woman should accept debilitating heavy periods as an inevitable feature of her menstrual life; effective, well-tolerated treatments exist across the spectrum of medical and surgical management, and the evidence base continues to grow.

The cornerstone of good clinical care is accurate diagnosis — identifying the specific underlying aetiology, whether structural (fibroids, adenomyosis, polyps), systemic (coagulopathy, thyroid disease), hormonal (anovulation, PCOS), or endometrial — and tailoring treatment to address the cause while respecting the patient's fertility wishes, lifestyle, and personal priorities. Iron deficiency must be actively sought and treated as a concurrent priority, not an afterthought. And the psychological and social burden of this condition must be acknowledged and addressed as an integral part of patient care.

For women experiencing heavy periods, the message is clear: this condition is common, it is treatable, and help is available. A conversation with a GP, gynaecologist, or specialist nurse is the first step toward effective management and a significantly improved quality of life.

Long-term data from the ECLIPSE trial confirm that both medical management with the LNG-IUS and surgical management with endometrial ablation or hysterectomy result in sustained improvements in health-related quality of life over 5 years, with the majority of women reporting that treatment has "changed their life." Early, appropriate treatment of heavy menstrual bleeding — rather than years of watchful waiting — is associated with the best outcomes for women's quality of life, fertility preservation, and long-term health.
