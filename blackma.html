Maternal Health & Science

Black Maternity & Pregnancy

Science, health disparities, and evidence-based care for better outcomes

Black women in the United States are 2–3 times more likely to die from pregnancy-related causes than white women — a disparity that persists across education levels, income, and access to care. This comprehensive, science-based guide examines the biological, systemic, and social factors driving Black maternal health disparities; the physiological nuances of pregnancy in Black women; evidence-based strategies for safer pregnancies; and the cutting-edge research reshaping maternity care. Understanding these issues is not only a matter of medicine — it is a matter of justice.

The Scale of the Crisis: Epidemiology of Black Maternal Mortality

Key Statistics

2.6× Higher maternal mortality rate for Black women vs. white women in the U.S. (CDC, 2023)
69.9 Maternal deaths per 100,000 live births among Black women (2021 data)
~60% Of maternal deaths considered preventable, regardless of race
3–4× Higher rates of severe maternal morbidity (SMM) for Black women

Maternal mortality is defined as death occurring during pregnancy or within 42 days of delivery from causes related to or aggravated by the pregnancy. Severe maternal morbidity (SMM) encompasses 21 life-threatening conditions including blood transfusion, hysterectomy, renal failure, and mechanical ventilation — events that, while non-fatal, represent near-misses and significant long-term health consequences.

The disparity in Black maternal mortality is not new. CDC surveillance data show this gap has persisted for decades and widened during the COVID-19 pandemic. Critically, studies controlling for income, education, and insurance status find the disparity narrows but does not disappear, pointing to structural and interpersonal racism as independent contributors beyond socioeconomic factors alone.

Global Comparisons

The U.S. has the highest maternal mortality rate among high-income nations. Black women in the UK face approximately 3.7 times the maternal death risk of white women. In Australia, Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander women experience maternal mortality rates approximately 2.6 times higher than non-Indigenous women. These cross-national patterns suggest that racial disparities in maternal health are not uniquely American but reflect broader systemic inequities in racially stratified healthcare systems.

Physiology and Pregnancy: What the Science Reveals

Cardiovascular Physiology in Pregnancy

Normal pregnancy imposes significant cardiovascular demands: cardiac output increases by 40–50%, blood volume expands by approximately 50%, and heart rate rises by 10–20 beats per minute. These adaptations begin in the first trimester and peak around 28–32 weeks of gestation.

Why These Changes Matter More for Black Women

Black women have higher baseline rates of hypertension, left ventricular hypertrophy, and arterial stiffness before pregnancy — partly due to the physiological effects of chronic stress (allostatic load) and partly due to documented undertreatment of cardiovascular risk factors. When the cardiovascular demands of pregnancy are superimposed on an already-stressed cardiovascular system, the risk of hypertensive disorders of pregnancy increases substantially. Preeclampsia affects approximately 5–8% of all pregnancies but occurs at higher rates — and more severe forms — in Black women.

Hypertensive Disorders of Pregnancy (HDP)

Hypertensive disorders of pregnancy encompass gestational hypertension, preeclampsia, eclampsia, and chronic hypertension with superimposed preeclampsia. They are the leading cause of maternal and perinatal morbidity and mortality in the United States.

Classification of Hypertensive Disorders in Pregnancy

Gestational Hypertension: BP ≥140/90 mmHg after 20 weeks; no proteinuria or other end-organ signs

Preeclampsia: Gestational hypertension + proteinuria OR end-organ dysfunction (renal, hepatic, neurological, hematological)

Severe Features Preeclampsia: BP ≥160/110, platelet count <100,000, creatinine >1.1 mg/dL, elevated liver enzymes, or neurological symptoms

Eclampsia: New-onset seizures in a woman with preeclampsia; a leading cause of maternal death

HELLP Syndrome: Hemolysis, Elevated Liver enzymes, Low Platelets — a severe variant affecting approximately 0.5–0.9% of pregnancies

Research published in Hypertension (2019) found that Black women with preeclampsia are significantly more likely to progress to severe disease and experience complications including stroke, pulmonary edema, and placental abruption compared to white women with the same diagnosis. A key factor is that Black women's elevated blood pressure in early labor is more often undertreated — a form of implicit bias documented in multiple hospital audits.

The Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone System (RAAS) and Race

The RAAS regulates blood pressure, fluid balance, and electrolyte homeostasis. During pregnancy, RAAS activity increases significantly to support expanded blood volume. Research suggests that some Black individuals carry variants in genes encoding angiotensinogen and aldosterone synthase that alter RAAS activity, potentially contributing to higher blood pressure responses under physiological stress.

Important Caveats on Race and Genetics

Race is a social construct, not a biological category. While population-level genetic variants exist that correlate with continental ancestry, "Black" or "African American" encompasses enormous genetic diversity. Using race as a proxy for specific genetic variants risks oversimplification and can reinforce harmful stereotypes. The appropriate approach is individual clinical assessment, not race-based assumptions. Pharmacogenomic testing for specific variants (e.g., ACE gene polymorphisms) is clinically more meaningful than race as a predictor of medication response.

Preterm Birth: Biology and Disparity

Preterm birth (before 37 completed weeks of gestation) affects approximately 10.5% of all U.S. births but occurs in roughly 14.4% of births to Black women — nearly 1.5 times the rate for white women. Very preterm birth (<32 weeks) shows even larger disparities.

Proposed Biological Mechanisms

Several pathways may contribute to increased preterm birth risk. Bacterial vaginosis, more prevalent in Black women due to a less protective vaginal microbiome composition (lower Lactobacillus dominance), is associated with ascending infection and preterm labor. Chronic inflammation — elevated in individuals experiencing chronic stress — can stimulate prostaglandin and cytokine release that initiates uterine contractions. Shorter cervical length, which predicts preterm birth risk, may have higher prevalence in Black women, though this is an active research area. Progesterone supplementation reduces preterm birth risk in women with short cervix and is an important evidence-based intervention.

The "weathering hypothesis," proposed by epidemiologist Dr. Arline Geronimus, suggests that chronic exposure to social and economic adversity accelerates biological aging in Black women. Markers of biological aging — including telomere length, inflammatory markers (IL-6, CRP), and allostatic load indices — are demonstrably higher in Black women of reproductive age compared to white peers of the same chronological age. This biological "weathering" contributes to worse pregnancy outcomes independent of traditional medical risk factors.

Structural Racism and Obstetric Care

Implicit Bias in Clinical Settings

Implicit bias refers to unconscious attitudes and stereotypes that affect decisions, actions, and understanding. In healthcare, implicit racial bias has been documented among physicians, nurses, and other clinicians across multiple specialties, including obstetrics and gynecology.

A widely cited study published in the Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences (2016) found that a subset of medical students and residents endorsed false biological beliefs about Black patients — including that Black people have "thicker skin" and "less sensitive nerve endings" — and that these beliefs correlated with less accurate pain treatment recommendations. These misbeliefs echo historical pseudoscientific claims and have direct implications for pain management during labor and postpartum care.

How Bias Manifests in Maternity Care

Research documents several patterns: Black women's pain reports are more often dismissed or undertreated during labor and the postpartum period. Black women are less likely to receive timely treatment for hypertensive urgencies in obstetric triage. Concerns raised by Black patients about symptoms are less likely to prompt immediate diagnostic workup. These patterns persist even when controlling for insurance status, hospital type, and clinical severity — pointing to interpersonal rather than purely systemic explanations.

Hospital Quality and Geographic Segregation

Because residential segregation remains pervasive in the United States, Black women disproportionately deliver at lower-resourced hospitals — facilities with fewer maternal-fetal medicine specialists, less access to blood banking, fewer intensive care unit beds, and lower volumes of high-risk deliveries.

Research from the New England Journal of Medicine (2019) estimated that eliminating within-hospital disparities would reduce Black-white SMM gaps by approximately 10%, while eliminating between-hospital disparities (ensuring equal access to high-quality hospitals) would account for the remaining 90% of the gap. This finding underscores that systemic, structural factors — not individual clinical interactions alone — drive the majority of the disparity.

Historical Context: Medical Exploitation and Distrust

The roots of Black distrust of the medical system are not irrational — they are historically grounded. J. Marion Sims, widely called the "father of modern gynecology," developed surgical techniques by operating on enslaved Black women without anesthesia, operating on the documented assumption that Black women did not feel pain as acutely as white women. The Tuskegee Syphilis Study (1932–1972), in which Black men with syphilis were deliberately left untreated without informed consent, remains a landmark case of medical abuse. Forced sterilizations of Black women, as late as the 1970s, further eroded institutional trust.

Clinical Implications of Medical Distrust

Medical distrust is a rational response to documented historical harm. It contributes to delays in seeking prenatal care, avoidance of recommended interventions, and reluctance to disclose symptoms. Clinicians who acknowledge this history — rather than dismissing distrust as irrational — build more effective therapeutic relationships. Culturally humble communication, shared decision-making, and patient-centered care models have demonstrated ability to reduce distrust and improve engagement with prenatal services.

Common Pregnancy Complications: Disproportionate Impact on Black Women

Gestational Diabetes Mellitus (GDM)

GDM affects approximately 6–9% of pregnancies overall, with higher rates observed in Black, Hispanic, and Asian women compared to white women. GDM involves insulin resistance that develops or is first recognized during pregnancy, resulting in hyperglycemia and associated fetal complications.

Pathophysiology and Race

During normal pregnancy, placental hormones including human placental lactogen, progesterone, and cortisol increasingly oppose insulin action, requiring the pancreas to produce 2–3 times more insulin to maintain euglycemia. Women with insufficient pancreatic reserve or pre-existing insulin resistance — as is more common in Black women partly due to chronic stress-induced cortisol elevation and adipose tissue distribution differences — are unable to compensate adequately. Uncontrolled GDM increases risk of preeclampsia, cesarean delivery, macrosomia, neonatal hypoglycemia, and long-term maternal diabetes.

Black women with GDM have a 70% lifetime risk of developing type 2 diabetes — higher than the 50% risk for white women with GDM — suggesting more pronounced underlying metabolic vulnerability. Postpartum follow-up with glucose testing is critically important but occurs in fewer than 30% of eligible Black women due to systemic and logistical barriers.

Placental Abruption

Placental abruption — premature separation of the placenta from the uterine wall before delivery — occurs in approximately 1% of pregnancies overall but is 40–60% more common in Black women. It is associated with hypertension, smoking, cocaine use, trauma, and prior abruption.

Mechanisms and Risk Amplification

Placental abruption likely results from decidual vascular disease — abnormal spiral artery remodeling during placentation that leaves the placenta susceptible to sudden pressure changes or thrombosis. Black women have higher rates of chronic hypertension (which impairs spiral artery remodeling) and higher rates of inadequate prenatal care attendance (which delays identification of risk factors). The result is a compounding of biological vulnerability and care access gaps.

Postpartum Hemorrhage (PPH)

PPH, defined as blood loss exceeding 500 mL after vaginal delivery or 1,000 mL after cesarean delivery, is the leading cause of maternal mortality worldwide. Black women in the U.S. experience PPH at higher rates and are significantly less likely to receive timely hemorrhage interventions.

PPH Risk Factors and the Disparity

Higher rates of uterine fibroids in Black women contribute to uterine atony (most common PPH cause). Higher rates of multiple gestations and prior cesareans increase risk. More commonly delayed or absent oxytocin administration at third stage. Implicit bias may delay recognition of clinical deterioration in Black patients. Lower likelihood of hospital-level hemorrhage protocols being consistently applied.

Uterine Fibroids: A Major Driver of Disparities

Uterine leiomyomas (fibroids) are benign smooth muscle tumors of the uterus. They are present in approximately 70–80% of Black women by age 50, compared to 40–50% of white women. Black women develop fibroids approximately 10 years earlier, have larger and more numerous fibroids, and experience more severe symptoms.

Fibroids and Pregnancy Outcomes

Large or submucosal fibroids increase risk of first-trimester miscarriage, preterm labor, placental abruption, malpresentation (breech, transverse), and postpartum hemorrhage. During pregnancy, fibroids can undergo "red degeneration" — a painful infarction triggered by rapid growth demands that exceeds blood supply — requiring hospitalization for pain management. The disparity in fibroid burden is partly genetic (HMGA2 gene variants more prevalent in women of African ancestry), partly epigenetic (relaxer use and other exposures), and partly related to differences in vitamin D levels.

Vitamin D deficiency — highly prevalent in Black women due to melanin's role in reducing cutaneous UV-induced vitamin D synthesis — is associated with larger fibroids and higher fibroid recurrence rates after surgical treatment. Adequate vitamin D supplementation during pregnancy (1,500–2,000 IU/day for deficient individuals) is recommended but remains underutilized.

Prenatal Care: Access, Adequacy, and Evidence-Based Practice

Disparities in Prenatal Care Initiation

The American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists (ACOG) recommends first prenatal visit in the first trimester (ideally by 10 weeks). Black women are more likely to initiate prenatal care in the second trimester or receive inadequate prenatal care overall — defined by the Kotelchuck Adequacy Index as fewer than the recommended number of visits relative to gestational age at initiation.

CDC data shows that approximately 76% of white women received first-trimester prenatal care in 2021, compared to 67.1% of Black women. Critically, even among Black women with college education and private insurance, prenatal care initiation rates lag behind comparable white counterparts — suggesting that race-specific barriers extend beyond socioeconomic factors to include implicit bias, transportation challenges, inflexible work schedules, and distrust of the healthcare system.

Components of High-Quality Prenatal Care

First Trimester (Weeks 1–12)

Confirmation of intrauterine pregnancy; dating ultrasound; complete blood count (CBC); blood type and Rh factor; rubella, varicella, hepatitis B, HIV, syphilis, gonorrhea, and chlamydia screening; urine culture; genetic carrier screening (cystic fibrosis, sickle cell, spinal muscular atrophy); nuchal translucency ultrasound and cell-free fetal DNA (cfDNA) screening offered; blood pressure baseline established; preeclampsia risk assessment (Black race is an independent risk factor per ACOG); low-dose aspirin (81 mg/day from 12–28 weeks) recommended for women at high preeclampsia risk.

Second Trimester (Weeks 13–26)

Anatomy ultrasound at 18–20 weeks; quad screen or integrated serum screening offered; gestational diabetes screening at 24–28 weeks with 50g glucose challenge test (followed by 3-hour 100g GTT if abnormal); repeat CBC and urine culture; assessment of fetal growth; blood pressure monitoring at each visit; cervical length screening for women with prior preterm birth or risk factors.

Third Trimester (Weeks 27–40)

Group B Streptococcus (GBS) vaginal-rectal culture at 35–37 weeks; repeat anemia screening; fetal movement counting; non-stress test (NST) or biophysical profile (BPP) for high-risk pregnancies; blood pressure monitoring intensified; fetal growth ultrasound for suspected macrosomia or growth restriction; delivery planning including discussion of birth setting and emergency preparedness.

Low-Dose Aspirin: A Critical Intervention

ACOG and the U.S. Preventive Services Task Force recommend low-dose aspirin (81 mg/day) starting at 12–28 weeks of gestation (optimally before 16 weeks) for women at high risk of preeclampsia. Black race is an independent risk factor in multiple risk stratification tools.

Meta-analyses demonstrate that aspirin reduces preeclampsia risk by approximately 24% overall, and by up to 40% when started before 16 weeks in high-risk women. Despite this robust evidence, studies show that low-dose aspirin is prescribed to only 60–70% of eligible high-risk patients — with Black women less likely to receive the prescription than white women with identical risk profiles.

Labor, Delivery, and the Postpartum Period

Cesarean Birth Rates and Informed Consent

Black women have higher cesarean delivery rates than white women (35.8% vs. 31.0%), yet paradoxically also have higher rates of obstetric interventions that increase cesarean risk while having lower rates of labor induction — a seemingly contradictory pattern explained partly by variation in how physicians manage labor in Black patients.

The Pain Management Disparity in Labor

Research consistently documents that Black women receive less neuraxial analgesia (epidural/spinal) during labor than white women, even after adjusting for clinical contraindications and patient preference. Studies using patient-reported data show that Black women are more likely to report having pain unaddressed or having to ask multiple times before receiving pain relief. The physiological consequences of inadequately managed labor pain include elevated catecholamines (which can reduce uterine perfusion), increased maternal exhaustion, and reduced ability to cooperate with pushing efforts.

The Postpartum Period: A Critical Window

Over half of maternal deaths in the U.S. occur after delivery — the majority in the 7–42 days postpartum. Black women face disproportionate risks during this period from late postpartum preeclampsia, cardiomyopathy, and thromboembolic events.

Peripartum cardiomyopathy (PPCM) — a potentially life-threatening form of heart failure developing in the last month of pregnancy or within five months postpartum — occurs at 3–4 times the rate in Black women compared to white women. The reasons are incompletely understood but may involve differences in prolactin metabolism, nitric oxide bioavailability, inflammatory milieu, and pre-existing subclinical cardiovascular dysfunction. Mortality from PPCM is also higher in Black women.

Postpartum Warning Signs Every Woman Should Know

Urgent (call 911): Chest pain or pressure; difficulty breathing; seizure; fainting; severe headache not relieved by acetaminophen; thoughts of harming self or baby

Urgent (call provider today): Blood pressure ≥150/100 at home; soaking more than one pad per hour for 2+ consecutive hours; fever ≥38°C (100.4°F); leg pain, swelling, or redness; wound that is opening, red, or draining; inability to urinate or urination that burns severely

Postpartum Mental Health

Postpartum depression (PPD) affects approximately 1 in 7 women overall. Black women experience PPD at similar or higher rates but are significantly less likely to be screened, diagnosed, or treated. Barriers include stigma within some cultural contexts, provider bias in screening (less likely to screen Black patients), and limited access to culturally competent mental health services.

Screening and Treatment

The Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS) is the validated tool recommended for universal screening at prenatal visits and 4–6 weeks postpartum. An EPDS score ≥10 warrants further assessment. First-line treatment for mild-to-moderate PPD is psychotherapy (especially cognitive behavioral therapy); moderate-to-severe PPD warrants pharmacotherapy with SSRIs (sertraline and paroxetine have the most lactation safety data). Black women are undertreated at each stage of this pathway.

Nutrition and Supplementation in Black Pregnancy

Folic Acid and Neural Tube Defects

Folic acid (400–800 μg/day) before conception and in the first trimester reduces neural tube defect (NTD) risk by 50–70%. Black women have lower NTD rates than white women, but this advantage should not lead to complacency — folate deficiency remains a risk in women with poor dietary intake or malabsorption.

Iron Deficiency Anemia

Iron deficiency anemia (IDA) is the most common nutritional deficiency in pregnancy worldwide. Black women have higher rates of IDA due to higher fibroid prevalence (causing chronic blood loss before pregnancy), dietary patterns, and higher rates of pica (consuming non-food substances, more prevalent under stress). IDA is associated with preterm birth, low birth weight, and postpartum hemorrhage.

Recommended iron supplementation in pregnancy is 27 mg/day elemental iron for all pregnant women. Women with confirmed IDA require therapeutic doses of 100–200 mg/day. IV iron infusion is an option for women who cannot tolerate or absorb oral iron. Screening with CBC at first prenatal visit and at 24–28 weeks is standard practice.

Vitamin D

Vitamin D deficiency (serum 25-OHD <20 ng/mL) affects an estimated 82% of Black pregnant women compared to 12–24% of white pregnant women. Melanin reduces UV-dependent cutaneous vitamin D synthesis, and dietary sources are rarely sufficient. Vitamin D deficiency in pregnancy is associated with gestational diabetes, preeclampsia, preterm birth, and neonatal hypocalcemia.

Current ACOG guidance recommends screening for vitamin D deficiency in Black pregnant women and supplementing with 1,500–2,000 IU/day if deficient (above the standard 600 IU/day prenatal recommendation). Despite this guidance, routine vitamin D screening is performed in fewer than 30% of prenatal practices in the U.S.

Omega-3 Fatty Acids and Preterm Birth Prevention

Supplementation with omega-3 long-chain polyunsaturated fatty acids (LC-PUFAs), particularly DHA and EPA, has emerged as a promising strategy to reduce preterm birth. A 2019 Cochrane review found that omega-3 supplementation during pregnancy reduces early preterm birth (<34 weeks) by 42% — a finding of particular relevance given elevated preterm birth rates in Black women.

Sickle Cell Disease and Trait in Pregnancy

Prevalence and Inheritance

Sickle cell disease (SCD) is an autosomal recessive hemoglobinopathy caused by a point mutation in the beta-globin gene (HbS). SCD disproportionately affects people of African, Mediterranean, Middle Eastern, and South Asian descent. In the United States, SCD affects approximately 1 in 365 Black births, while sickle cell trait (one copy of HbS) occurs in 1 in 13 Black individuals.

Pregnancy Complications in Sickle Cell Disease

Pregnancy significantly increases the frequency of painful vaso-occlusive crises due to the hypercoagulable state, expanded blood volume, and physiological stress of pregnancy. Women with SCD have substantially increased risks of preeclampsia (3–4× baseline), preterm birth (2–3× baseline), fetal growth restriction, stillbirth, and maternal death. Management requires high-risk obstetric care, hematology co-management, hydroxyurea discontinuation before conception (teratogenic), and careful pain management during crises.

Sickle Cell Trait in Pregnancy

Historically considered benign, sickle cell trait has been associated in multiple studies with increased risk of urinary tract infections (particularly pyelonephritis), hypertensive disorders of pregnancy, pulmonary embolism, and fetal growth restriction. Screening at first prenatal visit is recommended for all Black women.

Evidence-Based Solutions and Advocacy

Doulas and Community Health Workers

Continuous labor support by trained doulas — non-clinical support persons who provide emotional, informational, and comfort measures throughout labor — is associated in Cochrane reviews with reduced cesarean rates, reduced pain medication use, and improved maternal satisfaction. For Black women, access to Black doulas who share cultural context and understand systemic racism in healthcare may be particularly beneficial.

A study published in Journal of Perinatology (2020) found that Black women who received doula support had preterm birth rates 22% lower than matched controls without doula care, and cesarean rates approximately 30% lower. Medicaid programs in several states now cover doula services specifically to address maternal health disparities — an evidence-based policy response.

Midwifery-Led Care Models

Freestanding birth centers and hospital-based midwifery programs that prioritize continuity of care, culturally humble communication, and shared decision-making have demonstrated improved outcomes for Black women. The "continuity of carer" model — in which women see the same midwife or small midwifery team throughout pregnancy — is associated with reduced preterm birth, reduced likelihood of epidural analgesia, and higher maternal satisfaction in meta-analyses.

Implicit Bias Training and Systemic Interventions

While evidence on implicit bias training alone as a standalone intervention is mixed, hospital-level systemic interventions have shown more consistent results. "Bundles" of standardized protocols — including mandatory blood pressure response algorithms, hemorrhage protocols requiring immediate action within specified timeframes, and mandated postpartum hypertension follow-up — reduce racial disparities in outcomes by reducing reliance on individual clinical judgment (where bias can operate).

Successful Systemic Programs

The California Maternal Quality Care Collaborative's hemorrhage and preeclampsia toolkits — standardized care bundles implemented statewide — contributed to a 55% reduction in maternal mortality from hemorrhage and a documented narrowing of racial disparities. New York State's Maternal Mortality Review Committees have identified implicit bias as a contributing factor in preventable deaths and mandated implicit bias training in hospitals with Medicaid contracts.

Advocacy: Knowing Your Rights

Rights and Advocacy Strategies for Black Pregnant Women

Ask direct questions: "What is my blood pressure reading?" "What is your plan if it goes higher?" Document your own readings.

Bring support: A doula, partner, or advocate who can speak up if your concerns are dismissed and who can document what is said.

Request written information: Ask for discharge instructions in writing; insist on understanding warning signs before leaving the hospital.

Know your hospital's patient rights policy: All U.S. hospitals receiving Medicare/Medicaid must have a patient advocate/ombudsman process.

Consider a high-risk center: If you have risk factors including hypertension, diabetes, fibroids, prior preterm birth, or SCD, ask for a referral to a center with maternal-fetal medicine specialists.

Breastfeeding: Benefits, Disparities, and Support

The Initiation Gap

Breastfeeding is associated with reduced rates of respiratory infections, otitis media, obesity, type 1 and type 2 diabetes, and SIDS in infants, as well as reduced maternal risk of breast cancer, ovarian cancer, type 2 diabetes, and cardiovascular disease. Despite these benefits, Black women in the United States initiate breastfeeding at rates approximately 15–20 percentage points lower than white women (64% vs. 84% in recent CDC data).

Barriers Specific to Black Women

Historical context matters: enslaved Black women were forced to breastfeed enslavers' children while their own infants went unfed — a traumatic legacy that persists in cultural memory. Contemporary barriers include return-to-work policies that do not protect pumping (disproportionately affecting Black women in hourly jobs), NICU admissions (higher in preterm Black infants), lower access to lactation consultants of the same racial background, and aggressive infant formula marketing historically targeted at Black communities. Hospital "Baby-Friendly" designation — requiring support for breastfeeding initiation — is less common at hospitals disproportionately serving Black women.

Research Landscape and Future Directions

Historical Exclusion from Clinical Research

Black women have been systematically underrepresented in clinical trials historically. The NIH Revitalization Act of 1993 mandated inclusion of women and minorities in NIH-funded research, but implementation has been uneven. Many obstetric interventions with demonstrated efficacy were initially tested in predominantly white populations — creating uncertainty about whether effect sizes generalize to Black women.

The Black Mamas Matter Alliance, National Birth Equity Collaborative, and similar organizations advocate for Black women-led research, Black maternal health researchers, and community-based participatory research models that center the expertise and lived experience of Black women in designing studies and interpreting results. This shift is gaining momentum, with NIH's IMPROVE (Implementing a Maternal health and Pregnancy Outcomes Vision for Everyone) initiative specifically targeting research gaps in maternal health disparities.

Telehealth and Remote Monitoring

Telehealth expansion during the COVID-19 pandemic demonstrated that remote prenatal visits are acceptable to most patients and may reduce geographic and transportation barriers disproportionately affecting Black women in rural and underserved areas. Remote blood pressure monitoring with connected apps — allowing daily reporting of readings and algorithmic escalation protocols — is being tested in clinical trials as a strategy to reduce missed hypertensive emergencies in the postpartum period.

Pharmacogenomics and Personalized Medicine

Emerging research in pharmacogenomics aims to identify genetic variants affecting drug metabolism relevant to pregnancy, including responses to labetalol, nifedipine, and magnesium sulfate — first-line treatments for hypertensive disorders of pregnancy. Tailoring dosing to individual genotype rather than relying on race-based generalizations represents the scientifically rigorous and equitable path forward.

A Note on Language and Dignity

Language in clinical and research contexts shapes how Black women experience care. The term "advanced maternal age" can feel stigmatizing; "maternal age ≥35" is neutral and clinical. Describing disproportionate health outcomes as evidence of biological racial difference — rather than as consequences of racism — is scientifically inaccurate and harmful. The scientific consensus, supported by the American Medical Association, the American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists, and the American Academy of Pediatrics, is that race is a social and political construct, not a biological one, and that racial health disparities are the product of racism, not race.

Ultimately, improving Black maternal health requires simultaneous action at multiple levels: individual clinicians confronting bias; hospitals implementing standardized protocols; health systems improving equitable access; insurers removing barriers to care; policymakers expanding Medicaid coverage and paid leave; and researchers centering Black women's experiences. No single intervention is sufficient. What is clear is that the current situation — a 2.6-fold disparity in maternal death in one of the world's wealthiest nations — is neither inevitable nor acceptable.
